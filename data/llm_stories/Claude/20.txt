The whispers started on a Wednesday.

Paul Devane lay in bed at 2:14 AM, staring at the ceiling, and heard them — faint, rhythmic, like someone reading aloud in another room. He held his breath and listened. The house was still. No television left on. No neighbors close enough to hear. Just the whispers, threading through the silence like smoke.

He'd been sleeping poorly since Rebecca died. Since Rebecca died — that was how he phrased it to himself, passive, accidental, as if she'd simply stopped. Not as if he'd held the pillow over her face while she slept and pressed until she stopped moving. Not as if he'd called 911 afterward and performed grief with the precision of a man who'd practiced in the mirror. Not as if the coroner had ruled it natural, cardiac arrest in sleep, case closed.

Two weeks. The house still smelled like her perfume. He hadn't washed her pillowcase. He told himself this was part of the performance — a grieving husband surrounded by his wife's things. But the truth was simpler: he couldn't bring himself to remove the last trace of her. Not out of guilt. Paul didn't feel guilt. Out of habit. Rebecca had been part of the architecture of his life, and without her, the rooms felt wrong.

The whispers grew louder.

He got out of bed. The hallway was dark, the floorboards cold under his bare feet. He stood and listened. The sound was coming from downstairs. From the kitchen.

Paul descended the stairs slowly. He didn't turn on the lights. He told himself this was confidence — he wasn't afraid. He was Paul Devane. He was in control.

The kitchen was empty. Moonlight lay in silver rectangles across the counter. The whispers had stopped.

Then they started again. Behind him.

Paul spun. Nothing. The hallway was empty. But the whispers were closer now, and he could almost make out words. A woman's voice. Low, steady, patient.

"...told you... knew you would... always knew..."

Rebecca's voice.

His chest tightened. Not possible. He'd confirmed the death himself. He'd watched them take the body. He'd attended the funeral, a closed-casket affair because he couldn't look at her face without remembering the way her hands had clawed at his wrists at the end.

"You're not here," he said to the empty kitchen.

The whispers stopped. Silence — thick, absolute, ringing.

Then, from directly behind his left ear, close enough that he felt breath that wasn't there:

"I never left."

Paul stumbled forward, crashing into the counter. He grabbed a knife from the block — reflex, absurd — and held it out in front of him. The kitchen was empty. The house was empty. He was alone.

But the air had changed. It was heavier now, warmer, carrying a scent he recognized. Her shampoo. Lavender and something underneath — metallic, like blood, like the smell of something wrong.

He searched the house. Every room, every closet, the basement, the attic. Nothing. No one. The whispers had stopped.

He went back to bed. He did not sleep.

In the morning, he found something on the bathroom mirror. Written in the condensation from the shower he hadn't taken, in handwriting he recognized — neat, left-slanting, the way she'd written grocery lists and birthday cards:

I KNOW WHAT YOU DID.

Paul stared at it until the condensation dried and the words vanished.

He sold the house three days later. He moved to another state. He changed his name.

But on certain nights, in certain silences, when the air grew heavy and warm and smelled of lavender, he heard her. Still whispering. Still patient.

Still waiting.
