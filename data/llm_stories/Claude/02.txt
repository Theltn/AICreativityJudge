The morning briefing at the Center for Decomposition Control was standing room only. Director Mara Holloway adjusted her jaw — it had come loose again during the commute — and addressed the room.

"We've confirmed three more cases in the Westside district. Living, breathing, warm-blooded humans. Full cardiac function. Uncontrolled cellular regeneration."

A murmur rippled through the assembled dead.

Officer Grim raised a hand, scattering a few knuckle bones. "How are they spreading?"

"We believe it's airborne," Mara said. "Something in their exhalations. They breathe constantly — in and out, in and out — and wherever they go, tissue starts repairing. We've had two confirmed reversions in the last forty-eight hours."

The room went silent. Reversion. The word no zombie wanted to hear. Perfectly decayed citizens waking up with pulses, flushed skin, the sudden horrifying urge to eat salads.

"My neighbor reverted last Tuesday," whispered someone in the back. "She started sleeping. Eight hours, every night. Just lying there. Not even moving."

Mara nodded grimly. "We understand the fear. But I want to assure everyone that the Ministry of Eternal Rest is working around the clock — well, around the perpetual overcast, you know what I mean — to contain this."

After the briefing, Mara walked through downtown, past the cafes where citizens sat motionless for hours in perfect contentment, past the parks where children played without ever getting tired or needing to stop, past the library where a woman had been reading the same book for eleven years and was thoroughly enjoying it.

This was civilization. Quiet. Patient. No one rushing anywhere. No anxiety, no heartburn, no existential dread about mortality. They'd solved all of that by dying.

And now the humans threatened everything.

She found the quarantine zone on Ashwood Street. Through the chain-link fence, she could see them — a pocket of the infected, huddled together. Their skin was horrifyingly smooth. Pink. They moved constantly, fidgeting, pacing, their eyes darting around as if the world were on fire.

One of them pressed against the fence. A young man, maybe twenty-five, his cheeks flushed with blood. Actual blood, moving through actual veins.

"Please," he said. "We're hungry."

Hungry. The concept was almost obscene. Zombies ate when they felt like it, which was occasionally, and they certainly never needed to.

"We'll send provisions," Mara said stiffly. She turned to Officer Grim. "Double the perimeter. And for Rot's sake, nobody breathe in." She paused. "Wait, do we breathe?"

"Force of habit, ma'am."

"Well, stop it."

That night, Mara sat in her office, staring at the epidemiological map. Red dots blooming across the city like wounds healing. The humans were spreading, and everywhere they went, they brought warmth, urgency, hunger, fear, desire, impatience — all the plagues of the living.

She picked up the phone and dialed the Minister of Eternal Rest.

"Sir, I think we need to consider full municipal quarantine."

"Is it really that bad?"

Mara looked out the window. Down in the street, two reverted citizens were arguing loudly about parking. One of them was crying.

"Sir," she said, "they've started having feelings."

The Minister gasped, and his left ear fell off.

"Mobilize everyone," he said.
