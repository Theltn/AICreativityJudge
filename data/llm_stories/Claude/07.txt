The ration line stretched three blocks, same as every morning. Fen stood near the back, hands in her pockets, watching the Compliance drones drift overhead like fat metal wasps. The line moved slowly. It always did.

The woman ahead of her was holding a child — two, maybe three years old, wrapped in a coat made from a sewn-together shower curtain. The child was quiet. Children in the Blocks learned early that noise drew attention, and attention drew drones.

The line shuffled forward. When the woman reached the distribution window, she held out her ration card. The scanner beeped red.

"Expired," the clerk said. "Next."

"It's not expired. I renewed it last week. Please check again."

"Card's expired. Step aside."

The woman didn't move. The child whimpered. A drone rotated, its camera eye clicking.

"Please," the woman said. "She hasn't eaten since yesterday."

"Step aside or I'll flag you for disruption."

The woman's shoulders caved. She stepped out of line, clutching her daughter, and stood against the concrete wall. She didn't cry. Crying was a disruption.

Fen reached the window. She held out her card. It beeped green.

"Two-day ration," the clerk said, sliding a gray package through the slot. "Next."

Fen took the package. It was the size of a paperback — compressed nutrient bars, a water purification tab, a vitamin strip. Enough for two days if she was careful. She calculated quickly: she had a quarter-bar left at home. If she skipped tomorrow's breakfast, she could stretch three days from two.

She walked to the woman against the wall.

"Here," Fen said, holding out the package.

The woman stared at her. "What?"

"Take it."

"I can't take your rations."

"You can. I've got extra at home." The lie was smooth. Fen had been hungry so long she barely noticed it anymore.

The woman's eyes filled. She glanced at the drone overhead, then back at Fen.

"They'll flag you for unauthorized transfer."

"They flag me for breathing wrong. Take it."

The woman took the package with shaking hands. Her daughter reached for it immediately, tiny fingers grasping.

"Thank you," the woman whispered. "Thank you. I'll find a way to —"

"Don't," Fen said. "Don't find me. Don't remember my face. Just feed her."

Fen walked away. The drone tracked her for half a block, then lost interest. She turned down an alley, leaned against a wall, and pressed her hand to her stomach.

Three days without food. She'd done it before. During the Consolidation, she'd gone five. The body adjusted. The mind was harder.

But the child's face — the quiet, patient hunger of a kid who'd already learned not to ask — that would stay with her longer than the emptiness in her gut. In a world designed to strip everything down to survival, the most radical thing you could do was give something away.

Fen walked home through the gray streets, past the checkpoints and the propaganda screens, past the shuttered schools and the barricaded parks. Her stomach growled. She ignored it.

Somewhere behind her, a little girl was eating.

That was enough.
