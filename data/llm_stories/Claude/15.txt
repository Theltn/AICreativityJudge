The cure arrived on a Thursday, delivered by an entity that never identified itself. It manifested as a broadcast — every screen, every speaker, every radio on Earth, simultaneously, for eleven seconds. A frequency, a molecular pattern, instructions for synthesis so simple a high school chemistry lab could produce it.

Death by natural causes was over. Cancer, heart disease, organ failure, neurodegeneration — all of it, reversible. One injection, derived from materials available in any pharmacy, and the body's cells would repair and regenerate indefinitely. Aging stopped. Disease stopped. The clock stopped.

Accidents could still kill you. Violence could still kill you. But time could not.

The first year was euphoria. The cure worked exactly as described. Hospitals emptied. Funeral homes closed. The global population held its breath, then cheered.

The second year, someone did the math.

Earth could sustain, by the most generous estimates, eleven billion people. The population was at eight billion and climbing. Without death by natural causes, the climb had no ceiling. Birth rates didn't slow — why would they? People were healthy, vital, fertile. A couple in their eighties could have children now. And they did.

By year three, the number hit nine billion. Resources tightened. Not catastrophically, not yet, but the curve was visible to anyone who looked. Freshwater. Arable land. Housing. Energy. All of it finite. All of it approaching limits that had previously been managed by the quiet, natural turnover of death.

The United Nations convened a special session. The debate was civil at first.

"We could expand," suggested the delegate from Brazil. "Desalination. Vertical farming. Space colonization."

"Those are decades away," replied the delegate from India. "The population crisis is years away."

The word no one wanted to say hung in the air like smoke. Someone from a Nordic country finally said it: "We need to discuss birth regulation."

The room exploded.

Reproductive limits. Licensing. Lottery systems. Every proposal was monstrous, and every proposal was necessary. Churches condemned it. Governments fractured over it. Protests erupted in forty countries.

Meanwhile, a quieter conversation was happening in living rooms and kitchen tables. Couples who had planned on two children now wondered if they should have any. Parents who were already raising families felt the weight of what they'd brought into a world that might not want more.

An economist named Dr. Adaora Obi published a paper that reduced the problem to a single equation: if birth rates held steady and no one died of natural causes, Earth would exceed its carrying capacity in fourteen years. Not might. Would.

The entity that had delivered the cure did not return. No guidance. No follow-up. Just the gift and the silence that came after.

In year five, the first Mortality Debates were held. Should the cure be reversible? Could someone volunteer to age again, to die naturally, to make room? A movement emerged — the Yielders — who argued that elective mortality was the highest form of generosity: choosing to end so others could begin.

An eighty-seven-year-old woman in Kyoto was the first to publicly refuse the cure. She held a press conference in a garden. "I have lived a full life," she said. "I would like to finish it. The world needs the space more than I need the time."

She died four months later, of pneumonia. Peacefully. The world watched.

Others followed. Not many. Not enough. The math was still merciless.

In a laboratory in Geneva, Dr. Obi stared at her projections and thought about the entity's gift. A cure for death, dropped into a world that ran on it. The most generous act imaginable, and the cruelest.

She wondered if the entity had known.

She suspected it had.
