Sir Aldric had rehearsed the moment a thousand times.

He'd kick down the door of the dragon's lair. He'd raise his father's sword — Oathkeeper, forged in the fires of Mount Cael, quenched in the blood of the last wyrm. He'd find the beast coiled around its hoard, scales gleaming like hammered copper, and he'd drive the blade home.

He'd been climbing for three days. The Dragon's Tooth was a mountain of black rock that pierced the clouds like a fang, its slopes littered with the bones of lesser knights who'd come before. Sir Aldric stepped over their rusted armor without looking down.

At the summit, the cave mouth yawned wide. The smell of sulfur rolled out in waves. Aldric drew Oathkeeper, its edge catching the thin mountain light. He stepped inside.

The lair was exactly as the legends described: a cathedral of stone, obsidian walls veined with gold, the floor carpeted in coins and gemstones. At its center, the dragon — Surathax the Nightfire, five hundred years old, wingspan like a galleon's sails — lay curled like a sleeping cat.

Aldric raised his sword.

And then he saw them.

In the curve of the dragon's tail, sheltered between its massive haunches, two small figures sat cross-legged on the stone floor. One was a child — his child, his son, Tam, missing for three weeks, the reason Aldric had climbed this mountain in the first place. The other was a dragon, barely larger than a hunting dog, its scales the pale green of new leaves, its wings folded neatly against its back.

They were holding hands.

Or rather, Tam was holding the young dragon's forepaw, and the young dragon was letting him, and they were both looking at a picture Tam had drawn in charcoal on the cave floor. It appeared to be a house. With two figures in front of it. One human-shaped. One dragon-shaped.

"Tam," Aldric said.

His son looked up. His face was dirty, his clothes torn, but his eyes were bright and unafraid. "Papa! You came!"

The great dragon opened one eye. It was the size of a dinner plate, amber and ancient and startlingly calm.

"I came to kill you," Aldric said to the dragon. The words sounded absurd now, but he said them because a knight says what he means.

Surathax lifted her head. When she spoke, her voice was low and warm, like coals shifting in a hearth.

"Your son wandered into my territory three weeks ago. He was lost, cold, and hungry. My daughter found him." She gestured with her enormous snout toward the young dragon. "They have been inseparable since."

Aldric looked at Tam. "You weren't captured?"

"No, Papa. Essie found me by the river. I was crying and she brought me here. Surathax gave me food. She's been teaching me to draw."

"Essie," Aldric repeated.

The young dragon — Essie — made a sound like a teakettle and pressed her snout against Tam's cheek. He giggled.

Aldric lowered his sword. It felt impossibly heavy in his hand. He'd spent three days climbing, fueled by rage and grief and the certainty that his son had been taken by a monster. And here was Tam, fed and warm and laughing, holding hands with a creature Aldric had sworn to destroy.

"The village thinks you took him," Aldric said.

"The village believes what it wants to believe about dragons," Surathax said. "They always have."

Aldric sheathed Oathkeeper. He sat on the cave floor across from his son and the young dragon. Essie watched him with iridescent eyes, wary but not afraid.

"Can Essie come home with us?" Tam asked.

Aldric looked at the dragon child. She tilted her head. Smoke curled gently from one nostril.

"I don't think the village is ready for that," he said.

Tam's face fell. Then Surathax spoke again.

"Perhaps," she said, "the village needs what it is not ready for."

Aldric thought about this for a long time. Then he held out his hand to the small green dragon. She sniffed it, sneezed a tiny spark, and placed her paw in his palm.

It was warm.
