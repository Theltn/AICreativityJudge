Let me tell you about Boyd Langtree.

Boyd was not a villain. Villains are dramatic. They have plans, ambitions, aesthetic choices. Boyd had none of these. Boyd was simply a man who understood exactly how much he could get away with and calibrated his entire existence to that line.

He managed a mid-size insurance company in Dayton. He was forty-four, fit in a way that suggested vanity rather than health, and he smiled constantly — a smile that never reached his eyes because his eyes were always calculating.

Boyd's gift was making you feel like the problem was you.

He denied claims with a warmth that made people thank him. He'd sit across from a woman whose house had burned down and say, "I completely understand your frustration, Janet. I do. And I wish I could help. But unfortunately, your policy has a sub-clause exclusion for electrical fires originating from pre-2010 wiring, and I just — my hands are tied. I hate it as much as you do." And Janet would leave thinking Boyd was on her side, that the real enemy was the policy, when Boyd had written the sub-clause himself.

He paid his employees just enough that they couldn't afford to quit. He praised them publicly and undermined them privately. He'd say things like, "Don't worry, I covered for your mistake at the meeting. Nobody even noticed." There had been no mistake. But now the employee owed him, and they'd spend weeks wondering what they'd done wrong.

He tipped fifteen percent — never more, never less. Enough to avoid judgment. Never enough to make a server's day.

He volunteered at the food bank on Thanksgiving, but only because his company's PR team documented it. He made sure to be photographed holding a ladle. He wore a flannel shirt he'd bought specifically for the occasion.

He called his mother every Sunday for exactly nine minutes. She interpreted this as devotion. It was scheduling.

He had a dog — a golden retriever named Biscuit — because a man with a golden retriever is a man you trust. Boyd did not particularly like Biscuit. He fed Biscuit on schedule and walked Biscuit on a route optimized for visibility in the neighborhood. Biscuit loved Boyd with the unconditional adoration of a creature incapable of detecting fraud.

The thing about Boyd was that he never broke a law. He never raised his voice. He never did anything you could point to in a courtroom or a therapist's office and say, "There. That's what he did." He operated entirely within the rules, because he'd spent a lifetime learning that the rules had gaps, and he could live very comfortably in those gaps.

His ex-wife once said, "Boyd isn't cruel. Cruelty requires passion. Boyd is just... efficient."

She'd left him after seven years. He kept the house. She kept the feeling that somehow, even though she'd initiated the divorce, she'd lost.

Boyd went to bed every night at 10:15 and fell asleep immediately. He never had trouble sleeping. He never had doubts.

That was the worst thing about Boyd Langtree. Not what he did, but that he slept perfectly well after doing it. Every single night. With Biscuit curled at his feet, loyal and oblivious, in a house he'd kept from a marriage he'd drained, in a life built on the precise mathematics of never quite being wrong enough to be caught.

You've met Boyd.

Everyone has.
