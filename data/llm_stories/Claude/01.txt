The Herald's Crossing shimmered into existence forty-seven AU past Pluto's orbit, a membrane of light that hadn't been there a moment before. Captain Adaeze Okonkwo gripped the armrest of the ISV Perseverance and stared.

"Full stop," she whispered. The ship's ion drives whined down to silence.

The membrane pulsed once — cobalt, then amber, then a color Adaeze had no name for. From its center emerged a vessel, if you could call it that. It looked like origami folded from starlight, angular and impossible, each facet reflecting a different constellation.

A voice filled the bridge. Not through the comms — through the walls, the air, the fillings in Lieutenant Park's teeth.

"Vessel of the third planet, fourth star-arm, you are approaching the boundary of Administered Space. Present your transit credentials."

Adaeze exchanged a glance with her first officer. Twenty-six years of planning. Fourteen years of travel. Three generations of fuel debt back on Earth. And now, a border guard.

"We don't have transit credentials," she said carefully. "We didn't know there was a border."

A pause. The origami ship rotated, and something scanned them — Adaeze could feel it moving through her bones like warm water.

"Species 4,011-C. Carbon-hydrogen. Pre-collective. No credentials on file. No embassy registration. No known trade affiliations." Another pause. "You are undocumented."

Park let out a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob.

"We're explorers," Adaeze said. "We've come from Earth. We just wanted to see what was out here."

"What is out here," the voice replied, "is Administered Space. Seventeen billion star systems under the Concordance Transit Authority. You cannot enter without documentation, a registered purpose, and a minimum liability bond of—" a burst of data crashed into their navigation computer, "—the equivalent of four hundred of your solar masses, converted to standard energy credit."

Adaeze closed her eyes. "We don't have that."

"No," the voice agreed. "You do not."

Silence stretched between the stars. The membrane behind the origami ship flickered with traffic — vast freighters, sleek diplomatic yachts, things that moved like living smoke. All of them passing through. All of them documented.

"Can we apply?" Adaeze asked. "For credentials. For registration. Whatever we need."

The voice considered this. "Application processing for a new species takes between forty and four hundred of your years, depending on bureaucratic load. You would need to remain in the Outer Waiting Zone." A coordinate appeared on their screen — a patch of empty space, cold and dark. "We can provide basic sustenance rations. Gravity is not guaranteed."

Adaeze looked at her crew. Fourteen years to get here. Fourteen years back. Or they could wait.

Park shrugged. "We packed board games."

Dr. Osei, the mission biologist, was already pulling up the application forms flooding their computer. She scrolled for a full minute before reaching the bottom.

"There are nine million fields," she said flatly.

Adaeze squared her shoulders, pulled up a chair, and began to type.

"Name of species," she read aloud. "Human." She looked at the membrane, the impossible traffic, the origami ship of a border guard who had just reduced humanity's greatest achievement to an immigration problem.

"Purpose of visit," she continued, and typed: To introduce ourselves to the neighborhood.

Somewhere in the dark between galaxies, a bureaucracy slowly turned its gears.
