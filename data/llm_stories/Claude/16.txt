The video had forty-seven million views.

In it, a man — lean, mid-thirties, unremarkable face — sat in a folding chair in what appeared to be his living room. He looked into the camera and said, "My name is Evan Marsh. I am immortal. I'm going to prove it now."

Then he picked up a revolver, pressed it to his temple, and pulled the trigger.

The footage showed everything. The recoil. The exit wound. Brain matter on the wall behind him. His body slumped sideways off the chair.

Then, over the next forty-five seconds — which the FBI's forensic team had verified was unedited, continuous footage — the wound closed. Tissue regenerated. Bone reformed. Evan Marsh sat up, blinked, wiped the blood from his face, and looked at the camera.

"Any questions?" he said.

The FBI had many.

Agent Dana Reeves sat across from Evan Marsh in Interview Room 3 of the J. Edgar Hoover Building. He was wearing a gray T-shirt spotted with his own dried blood. He seemed relaxed.

"Mr. Marsh, before we go any further, I need to inform you that discharging a firearm within District limits is a felony."

"I'll do the time," Evan said. "I've got plenty of it."

Reeves opened her file. "The video. Our analysts have confirmed it hasn't been edited. We've also reviewed the thirteen previous videos you posted over the last two years — drowning, immolation, a fall from a twelve-story building. All verified authentic. You have died on camera fourteen times."

"Fifteen. The first one was on a platform that got taken down."

"How?"

"I fell into a wood chipper at a county fair. It was actually an accident. The video just happened to—"

"I mean how are you immortal, Mr. Marsh."

Evan shrugged. "I don't know. I've been alive since 1743. I was born in a village in Wales. I've died — really died, heart stopped, brain activity zero — somewhere around four hundred times. Hanging, stabbing, disease, two wars, a lightning strike, and one incident with a horse that I'd rather not discuss. Every time, I come back. Takes between thirty seconds and a few hours, depending on the severity."

"And you're coming forward now because...?"

"Because I'm tired of hiding. I've been hiding for two hundred and eighty years. New names, new cities, new identities every couple of decades. It's exhausting. I thought if I proved it publicly, maybe I could stop running."

Reeves studied him. He didn't look like a man who'd been alive for centuries. He looked like a man who hadn't slept well.

"What do you want, Mr. Marsh?"

"I want a Social Security number that doesn't expire. I want to stop moving every fifteen years. I want to see a doctor who won't report me to a government agency." He paused. "I also want to stop being studied. I know that's coming. Labs and needles and 'just one more test.' I've been through it before. The British tried in 1812. The Russians tried in 1943. I escaped both times, and I'll escape again if I have to."

"We're not going to—"

"Yes, you are. You're already thinking about it. What makes him tick? Can we replicate it? Can we weaponize it?" He looked at Reeves with ancient eyes. "The answer is no. I've been poked and scanned and dissected — literally dissected, Agent Reeves, awake — and no one has ever found anything different about me. I'm biologically human in every way. I just don't stop."

The room was quiet. Reeves closed her file.

"Off the record?" she said.

"Sure."

"Is it lonely?"

Evan looked at the two-way mirror. His reflection stared back — a face that had seen the Industrial Revolution, two World Wars, the moon landing, the internet, and the inside of an FBI interview room that smelled like burnt coffee.

"You have no idea," he said.
