Every Thursday at 7 PM, cell block D held a Dungeons & Dragons game in the common room.

The guards thought it was hilarious. Five grown men rolling dice and pretending to be elves. Officer Haines called it "nerd therapy" and never watched too closely, which was exactly the point.

Marcus — "Shadowfen the Rogue" — was the Dungeon Master. Former accountant, in for embezzlement, and the smartest man in the block. He'd built the campaign over six months, and every word of it was a blueprint.

"Okay," Marcus said, spreading the hand-drawn map across the table. "Your party stands at the entrance to the Iron Citadel. There are four guard towers." He tapped each one. "North, south, east, and west."

Ray — "Thundar the Barbarian" — squinted at the map. "The north tower. That's the one near the armory, right?"

"Near the armory, yes. But the armory is locked. Two sentries on rotation. They switch every thirty minutes."

Ray nodded slowly. In the game, the armory was a treasure vault. In reality, it was the maintenance closet near the north stairwell, and the "sentries" were Officers Dahl and Petrov, who rotated shifts at 10 PM and 10:30 PM.

"What about the east passage?" asked Luis — "Brother Aldric the Cleric." Luis was in for armed robbery and had mapped every ventilation duct in the building during his work detail in HVAC maintenance.

"The east passage is narrow. One man at a time. It leads to the outer wall, but there's a portcullis — a gate — that drops at midnight."

"The portcullis locks from outside?" Luis asked.

"From outside. But Brother Aldric, with your knowledge of divine mechanisms" — lockpicking — "you could open it in under a minute."

Luis smiled. "I've been practicing my prayers."

Jerome — "Kira of the Shadows" — leaned in. He was the youngest, in for assault, and he'd spent three months building trust with the kitchen staff to get assigned to the loading dock. "My character has been scouting the merchant road. The supply wagons come through the east gate at dawn."

"Correct. The wagons are unchecked once they pass the outer wall."

"So if we hide in the wagons—"

"Your characters would need to reach the wagon staging area before dawn. That means moving through the east passage, past the portcullis, across the courtyard, and into the staging barn. Total distance: about six hundred paces. You'd have a window of roughly forty-five minutes."

DeShawn — "Ironveil the Paladin" — had been quiet. He was the muscle, the insurance. If something went wrong, DeShawn would make sure they still got through. "What about the golem?" he asked.

The golem was the security camera at the junction of C and D corridors. Marcus had timed its rotation. Fourteen-second blind spot every ninety seconds.

"The golem patrols a fixed route. If you time your movement, you can pass unseen. But if the golem spots you, the entire citadel goes to alert. Game over."

The five men stared at the map. It was beautiful — detailed, annotated, marked with symbols that looked like fantasy runes but were actually indicators for camera angles, door codes, and shift changes.

"So," Marcus said. "The campaign finale is two weeks from now. The stars align on the first of the month. Everyone needs to have their characters fully prepared."

Officer Haines glanced over from his station. "Who's winning?"

Marcus looked up with an easy smile. "Nobody yet. But the final dungeon is going to be one for the books."

Haines chuckled and went back to his phone.

Under the table, Marcus folded the map into a precise square and slipped it into the spine of the Player's Handbook. Two weeks. Every piece in place.

Roll for initiative.
