The fishermen off Baker Beach saw him first.

A shape in the water, just past the breakers. Too large for a seal, too still for a swimmer. It rose slowly, vertically, as if being pushed up from below by some unseen hand. Water cascaded off a massive frame — shoulders first, then a barrel chest, then a black beard so long it had become an ecosystem. Small crabs clung to it. Kelp threaded through it like ribbon.

The man stood waist-deep in the Pacific and looked at San Francisco with eyes that had not seen light in a century.

Grigori Yefimovich Rasputin took a breath. It tasted like gasoline and sourdough.

"Hm," he said. "Different."

He waded ashore. A jogger stopped, stared, and dropped her water bottle. Rasputin picked it up, examined it, bit the cap off, and drank. He handed it back.

"Spasibo," he said.

By noon, he was trending.

The first videos were shaky — a barefoot giant in waterlogged robes walking up Divisadero Street, pausing to study parking meters with the intensity of a jeweler. He put coins in three of them before someone told him they were digital now. He stared at the phone they showed him as if it were witchcraft, which, by his reckoning, it probably was.

By 2 PM, a crowd was following him. He hadn't spoken much — his English was thick, his Russian archaic — but he radiated a gravitational pull. People wanted to be near him. They always had.

A reporter reached him outside a coffee shop in the Haight. "Sir, can you confirm — are you actually Rasputin? The Rasputin?"

He considered the question. "They poisoned me," he said slowly, working through the English. "Then they shot me. Then they beat me. Then they put me in the river." He paused. "The river was cold. I walked along the bottom for a very long time. Then the bottom became the ocean. The ocean was also cold. I kept walking."

"You walked along the ocean floor for a hundred years?"

"I had much to think about."

The internet divided immediately. Half the world declared him a hoax — a performance artist, a viral marketing campaign for something. The other half noted that the DNA sample he'd willingly provided matched tissue recovered from a bloodstained shirt in a St. Petersburg museum. His dental records, pulled from Tsarist-era archives, were a perfect match. He was either Rasputin or the most elaborate fraud in human history.

The FBI invited him for a conversation. He agreed, on the condition that they feed him first. He ate eleven plates of food at a diner in the Financial District and declared American bread "a tragedy."

At the field office, Agent Marlowe sat across from him. "What do you want?" she asked.

Rasputin thought about this for a long time. Water still dripped from his beard onto the table.

"I was a man of appetites," he said finally. "I wanted power. I wanted influence. I wanted the ear of the Tsar and the company of his wife and the fear of his ministers." He looked out the window at the skyline — glass towers, suspension bridges, a drone delivering a package. "I walked the bottom of the ocean for one hundred years. In the dark. In the cold. With nothing but my thoughts."

"And?"

"My thoughts were not good company." He turned back to Marlowe. "I would like a warm room. Some bread — better bread than you have here. And perhaps a dog. I have never had a dog."

"That's it?"

He smiled. It was a strange smile — ancient, tired, a little bit broken, but genuine in a way that made Marlowe's skin prickle.

"I have been dead for a century, Agent Marlowe. The only thing I want now is to be alive very quietly."

They gave him a studio apartment in the Sunset District. He adopted a dog — a mutt named Sasha — and was seen each morning walking her along Ocean Beach, his enormous frame silhouetted against the fog, the Pacific at his back, the century behind him, walking finally in a direction that wasn't the dark.
