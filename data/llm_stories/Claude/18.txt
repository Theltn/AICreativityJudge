"Are you alright?"

"No, it's ringing."

Dr. Lena Moss pressed the otoscope deeper, uselessly. The ringing in Patient 7's left ear had started seventeen days ago and had not stopped. It was not tinnitus. Tinnitus was random, chaotic, the auditory system misfiring. This was structured. This had rhythm. This had, as Patient 7 insisted with increasing desperation, meaning.

"It sounds like a phone," he said. His name was David. He was thirty-one, a sound engineer, and he had not slept in six days. "An old phone. Rotary. Ringing in patterns. Three rings, pause, two rings, pause, three rings."

"David, there's nothing physically—"

"I know there's nothing physically wrong. I've had four MRIs. I've had my ears flushed, irrigated, and examined by three different ENTs. Nobody finds anything. But it's ringing, Dr. Moss. Something is calling me."

She referred him to psychiatry. He went. The psychiatrist found no psychosis, no delusion, no history of auditory hallucination. David was sane, rational, and hearing a telephone ring inside his skull.

On day twenty-two, he answered it.

He was sitting in his apartment, alone, at 3 AM. The ringing had escalated — louder, more insistent, the pattern tighter. Three-two-three. Three-two-three. He pressed his palm against his left ear and whispered, "Hello?"

The ringing stopped.

A voice spoke. Not through the air. Not through bone conduction. Directly into the auditory cortex, bypassing every physical mechanism of hearing, as if someone had plugged a cable into his brain.

"Finally," the voice said. It was female, old, tired. "I've been trying to reach you for weeks."

"Who is this?"

"That's complicated. The short version is: I'm the part of you that remembers what you were before you were born."

David's hand was shaking. "That doesn't make sense."

"No, it doesn't. I apologize. Communication through the vestibular nerve was not my first choice, but your species has very limited hardware." A pause. "I need you to listen. You're going to receive a series of sounds — frequencies, patterns, harmonics. Your training as a sound engineer is why you were selected. You'll understand them intuitively. I need you to transcribe them."

"Transcribe them into what?"

"Music. The closest analog your mind can process. The information I'm sending is a message — a warning — encoded in a format that predates your language by several billion years. Music is the only human system complex enough to carry it without data loss."

"A warning about what?"

"I can't say. That's part of the encoding problem. I can only send the signal. You have to decode it. But David — it's important. It's the most important thing."

The voice went silent. The ringing did not return.

Instead, David began to hear music.

It came in fragments — a melody here, a harmonic structure there, rhythm patterns that felt less composed than discovered. He sat at his workstation and began to write. The music poured out of him like water through a crack. He worked for fourteen hours without stopping.

When he finished, he had a composition. Eleven minutes long. Orchestral, but not quite — there were intervals that didn't exist in Western music, chords that bent in directions he had no notation for. It was beautiful in a way that hurt, like looking at something too bright.

He played it back.

The speakers hummed. The room vibrated. And in the final thirty seconds, David heard something underneath the music — a word, repeated, layered in harmonics so deep they registered as feeling rather than sound.

He couldn't translate it. But he understood it perfectly.

He called Dr. Moss the next morning.

"Are you alright?" she asked.

"No," he said. "But I think I've finished the call."
