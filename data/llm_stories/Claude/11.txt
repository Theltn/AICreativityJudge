I found the door on a Tuesday.

I'd been working at Fantasia Animation Studios for three years — junior background artist, floor seven, desk by the window. It was a dream job, the kind people kill for. The campus had fountains shaped like cartoon characters, a cafeteria that served free everything, and hallways lined with original cels from films that had defined childhoods across six decades.

The door was in Sub-basement 3. I was down there looking for archived reference boards when I heard singing. Not recorded singing — live singing, muffled, coming from behind a wall that shouldn't have had anything behind it.

I found the seam. Pushed. The wall swung inward.

The room beyond was enormous — a warehouse, really, climate-controlled, humming with fluorescent light. And inside it, in rows of glass enclosures, were the characters.

Not drawings. Not puppets. Living, breathing, three-dimensional versions of every character Fantasia Studios had ever created.

In the nearest enclosure, a cartoon fox — Renny, from the 1987 film The Wishing Wood — sat on a small bed reading a paperback. He looked up at me with those enormous amber eyes, the ones that had made a generation of children cry during the goodbye scene.

"Oh," he said. "A new one."

I couldn't speak.

"It's okay," Renny said. "They all freeze up the first time."

Farther down, I saw Princess Lumina from Starfall, pacing her enclosure in her signature blue gown, her sparkle-trail growing dim as she walked. Captain Redbeard was doing push-ups. The villain Mordechai was sleeping.

A woman in a white lab coat appeared beside me. Dr. Nance, head of what was euphemistically called the "Performance Division."

"You're not supposed to be down here," she said calmly.

"What is this?"

"This is how we make our films." She said it the way someone explains where coffee comes from. "The characters are real. They always have been. The studio's founders discovered how to bring illustrations to life in 1953. Every character you've ever seen on screen was performed by the original — captured by our proprietary rendering system and translated to two-dimensional animation."

"They're prisoners."

"They're employees. They have contracts."

I looked at Renny. "Do you have a contract?"

Renny held up a sheet of paper. It was written in a language that didn't exist — character-script, Dr. Nance explained, a linguistic system the characters had developed among themselves. The contracts were in it because the characters couldn't read English. They'd signed documents they couldn't understand.

"They're fed, housed, and given recreation time," Dr. Nance said. "They're treated well."

Princess Lumina pressed her hands against the glass. Up close, I could see the wear on her — colors slightly faded, edges softer than they should have been. She'd been here since 1994.

"Please," she said. Her voice was exactly the way it sounded in the film — musical, layered with light. "Tell someone."

Dr. Nance took my arm. "Let me show you the NDA you'll be signing."

I signed it. I had student loans and a lease and a career I'd spent a decade building. I signed it and went back to desk seven and drew backgrounds and tried not to think about the fox reading paperbacks underground.

But I think about it every day. And I'm writing this because NDAs expire, and mine expires tomorrow.

Renny, if you're still down there — I'm sorry it took so long.
