Yara held the lantern higher and watched the children's faces.

They stood at the edge of the Surface Gate — the first group allowed up in six years. Twelve kids, ages seven to thirteen, born in the Undercity, raised under four hundred feet of rock and recycled air. None of them had ever seen the sky.

The gate groaned open. Gray light poured in, and the children went still.

Yara remembered her own first time on the surface, twenty years ago, when there had still been color. Green hills. A river that caught the light. Now there was only the Wash — miles of pale dust and skeletal trees under a sky the color of old bone. The sun hadn't broken through in eleven years. The Veil, they called it. No one knew if it would ever clear.

But the children didn't know what they were missing.

Kael, the youngest, stepped forward first. He looked up, and his mouth fell open. He stood like that for a long time, chin tilted toward the featureless white ceiling of cloud that stretched in every direction.

"It doesn't end," he whispered.

Yara felt something crack in her chest. "No," she said. "It doesn't."

"Is that the sky?" asked Lina, gripping her brother's sleeve.

"That's the sky."

Lina started crying. Not from sadness. From the sheer overwhelming volume of it. She had grown up in corridors. The biggest space she'd ever known was the Undercity's central cavern, which was the size of a stadium. The sky was the size of everything.

The children spread out across the Wash. Kael picked up a handful of dust and let it fall through his fingers, fascinated. "It's soft," he said. "The ground is soft."

They were used to metal floors and concrete. Yara watched him dig his hands into the earth like it was treasure.

Omar, thirteen and trying hard to be unimpressed, discovered the wind. It came in low across the flats, barely a breeze, but it moved his hair, and he froze. In the Undercity, air moved through vents in predictable currents. This was different. This was alive.

"Something's touching me," he said, alarmed.

"That's wind."

"It goes wherever it wants?"

"Yes."

He stood in it with his arms outstretched, letting it push against his palms, a grin breaking across his face despite himself.

Yara sat on a crumbled wall and watched them explore the wasteland like it was paradise. They found a puddle of gray water and celebrated it like an ocean. They found a dead tree and climbed it, marveling at the way it branched, so unlike the straight steel columns of home. They found a beetle — one of the few insects that had survived — and gathered around it in a reverent circle.

"It's alive," Kael breathed. "And it's just walking around. Nobody's taking care of it."

Yara pressed her hand against her eyes.

Look at these kids. They grew up in a world without hope. Without sun. And everything here — every broken, dusty, depleted thing — was a miracle in their eyes.

She had spent years mourning what the surface used to be. The children didn't mourn. They didn't know to. They took the world as it was and loved it with the full force of their amazement.

When it was time to go back down, Kael tugged her sleeve. "Can we come back tomorrow?"

"Maybe," she said. "Soon."

He looked up at the white sky one more time. "I'm going to draw it," he said. "So I don't forget how big it is."

Yara took his hand and led them home, back under the rock, carrying with her the terrible, beautiful weight of knowing that wonder doesn't need a perfect world. It just needs new eyes.
