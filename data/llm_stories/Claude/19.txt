The mannequin was Jake's idea.

He found her at a thrift store — full-size, female, bald, one hand missing. He named her Deborah. He put her in the hallway of the apartment he shared with three roommates and sent the group chat a single rule: every day, someone has to pose Deborah differently.

It became the best part of living there.

Monday: Kyle dressed Deborah in a business suit and put a briefcase in her remaining hand. She stood by the front door as if leaving for work.

Tuesday: Priya posed her mid-yoga, balanced on one foot, arms extended. She duct-taped a headband around Deborah's smooth scalp.

Wednesday: Amara went full crime scene — Deborah lying on the kitchen floor, outlined in masking tape, a ketchup stain on her blouse.

Thursday: Jake posed her sitting at the dining table with a bowl of cereal and a newspaper, as if she'd lived there longer than any of them.

It was stupid. It was joyful. They looked forward to it every day.

On Friday, it was Kyle's turn again. He came home late from a shift, around midnight. The apartment was dark. He dropped his keys on the counter and walked toward his room.

There was a figure in the hallway.

Kyle glanced at it. Deborah, posed standing against the wall, arms at her sides, head tilted slightly left. He didn't remember anyone mentioning a new pose in the group chat, but it wasn't his turn to think about it. He walked past, close enough that his sleeve brushed the figure's arm.

It was warm.

Kyle stopped. He turned around.

The figure was still there. Same pose. Arms at sides. Head tilted.

But it wasn't Deborah.

Deborah was behind this person. Kyle could see her — the real Deborah, the thrift store mannequin — propped in the corner of the hallway near the bathroom, right where Amara had left her after the crime scene bit. Same bald head. Same missing hand.

The person in the hallway was a real person. Standing exactly like a mannequin. Wearing one of Deborah's outfits — the business suit from Monday. Not breathing visibly. Not moving.

Kyle's blood went cold.

He took a step back. The figure did not react. He took another step. Nothing. He pulled out his phone, hands trembling, and turned on the flashlight.

The figure was a woman. Young — mid-twenties. Her eyes were open but fixed, staring straight ahead, glassy and unblinking. Her skin was pale. She stood with a stillness that was not human, the kind of stillness that required conscious effort, every muscle locked.

Then she blinked.

Kyle screamed.

The woman bolted. She moved so fast that Kyle barely registered direction — a blur toward the living room, the sound of the window sliding open, and she was gone. Down the fire escape. Into the dark.

Kyle hit the lights. The apartment was empty. Nothing was stolen. Nothing was disturbed. The window had been forced open with a flathead screwdriver, the marks fresh on the frame.

The police came. They took a report. They dusted for prints. They found several — none in the database.

Jake disabled the locks and installed a deadbolt the next day. Priya bought a security camera. Amara moved Deborah into the closet and didn't take her out again.

They never found the woman. She never came back. But for months afterward, Kyle would walk down the hallway at night and hesitate, counting the shapes in the dark, making sure there was one fewer than he saw.

Deborah eventually went back to the thrift store. Nobody posed her again.

But sometimes, late at night, Kyle would look at a coat hanging on a door or a shadow standing in a corner, and his heart would stutter, and he'd remember the warmth of a stranger's arm in the dark, pretending to be something that wasn't alive.
