"Seatbelts, everyone!" Mrs. Frizzle sang out, and the Magic School Bus shuddered, shrank, and plunged through Carlos's left nostril before anyone could object.

The plan had been simple: explore the human immune system, watch some white blood cells do their thing, write a report. Standard Frizzle fare. But as the bus spiraled down through the nasal cavity and into the bloodstream, Liz — Mrs. Frizzle's lizard — made a sound no one had heard before. A low, urgent chirp.

Mrs. Frizzle glanced at the navigation console. Her face did something it had never done in the entire history of Walkerville Elementary field trips.

It went pale.

"Oh my," she said.

"What is it, Ms. Frizzle?" asked Ralphie from the back.

"Well, class, it appears I've made a small miscalculation regarding our entry vector."

Arnold gripped his seat. "How small?"

The bus lurched sideways, caught in a current of rushing fluid. Outside the windows, the cellular landscape had changed. This wasn't Carlos's bloodstream anymore. The structures were wrong — too dense, too organized, humming with an energy the bus's instruments couldn't identify.

"We're not in Carlos," Mrs. Frizzle said quietly.

"Then where are we?"

Mrs. Frizzle enlarged the navigation display. The bus was a blinking dot inside a biological schematic that extended in every direction, impossibly complex. "It appears that when we entered Carlos's nasal cavity, we passed through a quantum biological membrane and into... well..."

"Into what?" Dorothy Ann demanded.

"Into everything, Dorothy Ann. We're inside the biosphere itself. The interconnected living system of the entire planet."

The bus drifted through a cathedral of mycorrhizal networks — fungal threads connecting root systems across what appeared to be miles of subterranean architecture. Wanda pressed her face to the glass. "Are those trees talking to each other?"

"They're sharing nutrients and chemical signals through a fungal internet," Mrs. Frizzle said, some of her enthusiasm returning despite the crisis. "It's a biological communication network that predates human civilization by four hundred million years."

"That's great," Arnold said. "How do we get out?"

"That's the miscalculation, Arnold. To exit, we need to return to the entry point — Carlos's nasal membrane. But I'm not entirely sure which direction Carlos's nasal membrane is from here. We could be under a forest in Oregon. We could be under the Pacific. The network is... extensive."

"You're lost," Keesha said flatly.

"I prefer 'temporarily without a fixed reference point.'"

The bus bumped against something, and the windows flooded with light. They'd entered a root bulb — an enormous, glowing chamber where nutrients were being processed by cells the size of beach balls.

"I knew I should have stayed home today," Arnold muttered.

"Arnold, we've been over this. Staying home is—"

"Perfectly reasonable when the alternative is being lost inside the planet's root system, Ms. Frizzle."

She couldn't argue with that.

Liz chirped again and pointed at the navigation screen with one scaly claw. A faint signal — Carlos's heartbeat, transmitted through the biological network like a sonar ping. Distant but detectable.

"Liz, you beautiful reptile," Mrs. Frizzle said. She gunned the engine. The bus rocketed through the network, following the heartbeat, weaving between fungal threads and root highways, past bacterial colonies and water tables and the sleeping bones of the earth.

They erupted from Carlos's left ear forty-seven minutes later, returning to full size in the middle of the school parking lot. Carlos sneezed violently.

"That," Mrs. Frizzle said, straightening her earrings, "was a teachable moment."

Arnold put his head between his knees.
