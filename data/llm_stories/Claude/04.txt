The leather straps bit into Marcus's wrists. The chair was cold — colder than he'd expected, though he wasn't sure what temperature he'd been hoping for. The gallery above was packed. He could hear them shifting in their seats, the rustle of coats, someone unwrapping a cough drop.

His lawyer had stopped making eye contact three days ago.

The chaplain leaned in. "Do you have any last words, son?"

Marcus thought about this. He'd rehearsed several versions. The righteous speech about innocence. The quiet dignity of saying nothing. The dramatic accusation. None of it mattered. The DNA was wrong, the witness was lying, and the jury had already gone home to sleep well.

"I didn't do it," he said. It came out smaller than he'd planned.

The warden nodded to the executioner — a man in a gray jumpsuit whose name, absurdly, was Mr. Flowers. Mr. Flowers checked the connections. He placed one hand on the switch.

Marcus closed his eyes. He thought about his mother's garden. Tomatoes in August. The way dirt smelled after rain.

Mr. Flowers flipped the switch.

The room went black.

Not metaphorically. Every light, every monitor, every humming piece of machinery in the building died at once. The emergency generator coughed and failed. In the gallery, someone screamed. Then several someones.

Marcus opened his eyes to perfect darkness. He was alive. His heart hammered against his ribs — proof, irrefutable proof, that the current had not reached him.

"Get the backup online!" the warden shouted.

"It is the backup, sir. Everything's down. The whole grid."

Flashlights clicked on, pale and jittery. In their beams, Marcus could see the faces of the witnesses — confused, frightened, strangely guilty, as if the universe had just called their bluff.

Minutes passed. Then an hour. Technicians came and went. The power refused to return. It wasn't just the prison; Marcus could hear fragments of radio chatter from the guards — the entire county was dark. A transformer had exploded at the substation. No one knew why.

The warden huddled with the state attorney in the hallway. Marcus could hear them through the open door.

"We can't just sit here with him in the chair."

"Procedure says we reschedule."

"For when? They're saying the grid might be down for days."

A guard came in and unbuckled the straps. Marcus's hands were shaking. The guard's were too.

They walked him back to his cell. The corridors were lit with emergency lanterns, orange and flickering, making the concrete walls look like the inside of a cave. Other inmates were pressed against their bars, watching him pass. A man Marcus had never spoken to reached through and touched his shoulder.

"God's watching," the man said.

Marcus didn't believe in God. But he sat on his cot in the dark and listened to the silence where electricity used to hum, and he thought: something is watching. Something saw me strapped into that chair for someone else's crime and said no.

Three weeks later, the real killer confessed. A routine traffic stop in another state. He'd been carrying the murder weapon in his trunk, and he broke down crying when the officer asked him to open it.

Marcus walked out of the prison on a Tuesday morning, into sunlight so bright it hurt. He stood on the sidewalk and breathed.

The power had come back on twelve hours after the execution failed.

No one ever explained why it went out.
