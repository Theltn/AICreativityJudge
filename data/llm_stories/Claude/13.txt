I woke up in a wheat field with a backpack and a headache.

The backpack contained: one printed copy of the English Wikipedia, circa 2024 — all 6.7 million articles, compressed into 1,200 pages of impossibly small type with a magnifying lens rubber-banded to the cover. Also a granola bar. I ate the granola bar first.

The year, as near as I could determine from the sun's position, the style of farming, and the soldier who found me two hours later, was 27 BC. I was somewhere in Roman Gaul.

The soldier brought me to a garrison commander. The commander spoke Latin. I spoke enough to say "I am a traveler" and "please don't kill me." He did not kill me. He gave me bread and watered wine and asked where I was from.

"Far away," I said. I clutched the Wikipedia to my chest like a baby.

I spent the first week learning how to not die. Wikipedia was helpful here. The article on Roman sanitation kept me from drinking unboiled water. The article on Roman social hierarchy kept me from accidentally insulting a patrician. The article on ancient currency helped me understand that I was worth exactly zero sesterces.

The magnifying lens saved my life. I demonstrated it to a local merchant, who thought it was sorcery. I let him believe that. He gave me a room above his shop in exchange for "more sorcery." I showed him the Wikipedia article on simple distillation and we built an apparatus from clay pots. The resulting alcohol was terrible but popular. I had income.

By the second month, I was reading the Wikipedia article on Roman concrete and weeping with joy. I knew the formula. I knew the ratios. The Romans already had concrete, but the article described improvements they wouldn't discover for decades. I approached a builder. He was skeptical until I showed him.

Here's what I learned: knowledge without context is almost useless. I had the chemical formula for penicillin, but I didn't have a laboratory. I had the schematics for a printing press, but I didn't have precision metalwork. I had the theory of flight on page 847, but I had no aluminum, no engine, no fuel.

What I did have was agriculture, medicine, and engineering principles that were centuries ahead. Small things. Crop rotation — the article was on page 203. I showed a farmer. He laughed. Then he tried it. Then he stopped laughing.

I showed a healer the Wikipedia entry on wound cleaning and basic germ theory. She couldn't read English, but I drew diagrams. Boil the water. Clean the instruments. She began doing it. Her patients stopped dying as often.

Word spread. By the sixth month, I was summoned to the provincial governor's estate. He wanted to know about the book.

I showed him the table of contents. He couldn't read it, but I translated the section headings. History. Science. Mathematics. Geography. Art. Philosophy. A map of the entire world, accurate to coastlines that wouldn't be charted for fifteen centuries.

He stared at the map for a very long time.

"Who wrote this?" he asked.

"Everyone," I said. "Thousands of people. Over many years. They wrote down everything they knew and gave it away for free."

He looked at me like I was insane. The concept of freely shared knowledge was more alien to him than the magnifying lens.

"And you," he said, "carry all of it?"

I held up the book. Twelve hundred pages. The accumulated knowledge of a civilization that wouldn't exist for two millennia, reduced to type so small it strained the eyes, and I was the only person alive who could read it.

"Yes," I said.

He offered me a position in his household. A salary. Protection.

I accepted. I opened the book to the chapter on aqueduct engineering and began to teach.

I couldn't change history. But I could make the water clean.
