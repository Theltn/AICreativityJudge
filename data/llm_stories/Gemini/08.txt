Let me introduce you to Gregory Vance.

Gregory doesn’t kick puppies. He doesn’t steal from orphans or hatch elaborate schemes to conquer the tri-state area. Villains of that caliber command a certain grudging respect for their ambition. Gregory is merely an Assistant Regional Manager for a mid-tier logistics firm, and it is entirely possible that he is the worst person on earth.

Gregory’s defining characteristic is a weaponized, aggressive pleasantness deployed to mask staggering incompetence and active sabotage.

You meet Gregory by the office coffee machine. He is in his late thirties, possessing a perfectly symmetrical face that lacks any trace of genuine emotion, like a well-rendered NPC in a cheap video game. 

"Morning, champ!" he says, clapping you on the shoulder just a fraction too hard. "Working hard or hardly working?" He uses phrases like this unironically, believing they constitute charm.

You tell him you were up until 3:00 AM fixing the quarterly report errors. Errors he made. 

"Ah, the report!" Gregory beams, showing perfectly white teeth. "Knew I could count on you to tidy that up. Teamwork makes the dream work, buddy. Hey, by the way, I sent an email CC'ing the VP praising 'our' combined effort on catching those slip-ups. You're welcome!"

Gregory steals credit with the brazen confidence of a seagull stealing a French fry. He doesn't do it maliciously; he genuinely believes that recognizing your hard work on his mistakes makes him a magnificent leader. 

He is the man who heats leftover salmon in the communal microwave on a daily basis, and when someone finally complains about the smell, he sends out a passive-aggressive department-wide email about "fostering a culture of culinary tolerance." 

If you bring a problem to Gregory, he will put on an expression of deep, tragic concern, furrowing his brow just so. "I hear you," he will say, nodding slowly. "I really do. We need to unpack this. Let's bookmark this conversation and circle back aggressively next quarter." He will then do absolutely nothing, allowing the problem to fester until it explodes, at which point he will step back and shake his head sadly. "I warned everyone we needed more bandwidth for this."

He replies-all to company-wide announcements with a single thumbs-up emoji. He parks his mid-sized luxury sedan straddling the line in the premium parking spots. He borrows your nice pen, specifically the one you like, chews on the cap, and then leaves it on Sarah’s desk.

But the most hateable thing about Gregory? The thing that makes you want to scream into the void? 

He is bulletproof. 

Upper management loves him. They see his meaningless buzzwords and relentless grinning as management material. He gets promoted. He gets the corner office. And he will forever remain convinced that he is the hero of the story, a beacon of positivity in a difficult world, blissfully unaware of the trail of migraines and clenched jaws he leaves in his wake. 

Gregory Vance is the friction in the universe made flesh. And he is going to ask you to chip in twenty dollars for a boss's day gift for himself.
