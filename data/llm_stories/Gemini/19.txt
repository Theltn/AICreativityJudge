The mannequin was an impulse buy from a defunct department store liquidation sale. We named him "Bartholomew." He was tall, faceless—just a smooth, beige oval where features should be—and possessed articulated joints that made posing him surprisingly easy. 

I lived in a cramped four-bedroom apartment with three friends: Dave, Sarah, and Chloe. Bartholomew became the fifth, silent roommate. 

The rule was established on day two: you had to pose Bartholomew in a new, creative scenario every day. If you walked past him and he hadn't moved in 24 hours, you were responsible for the next pose.

It started innocently enough. Bartholomew sitting on the couch holding a controller, 'playing' Xbox. Bartholomew peering out the kitchen window with binoculars. Bartholomew slumped over the toilet in a convincing display of synthetic intoxication. 

It became a competitive sport. We bought him thrift store clothes. We gave him props. I once came home to find him suspended from the ceiling fan, wearing a cape. 

Tuesday was my turn. The apartment was dead quiet. Sarah and Chloe were at night classes, and Dave was working a late shift. I was bone-tired from my own shift at the bakery. I keyed open the front door and threw my heavy bag onto the entryway table. 

I walked down the long, narrow hallway that connected the bedrooms to the living room. 

Bartholomew was standing right in the middle of the hallway. 

He was wearing Dave’s oversized grey hoodie, the hood pulled up over his featureless head. His hands were stuffed into the front pocket. He was positioned perfectly still, facing me, blocking the path.

I groaned, too tired to appreciate the subtle menace of the pose. "Nice try, Dave," I muttered to myself. It was a minimal effort pose, relying on lighting and placement rather than props. Lazy. 

I didn't stop walking. The hallway was narrow, so I turned my shoulder slightly to brush past him.

As I slid past, my arm grazed his shoulder through the fabric of the hoodie. 

It wasn't hard plastic. 

It was soft. 

And underneath the fabric, I felt the distinct, undeniable warmth of a living body. 

I froze, one step past the figure. My heart executed a violent stutter-step, hammering against my ribs. Rational thought vanished, replaced by an icy, primal panic. 

Mannequins don't radiate heat. Mannequins don't yield slightly when bumped. Mannequins don't breathe, and suddenly, in the suffocating silence of the hallway, I could hear a slow, ragged inhalation coming from the figure behind me.

I didn't turn around. I couldn't. I stood completely rigid, staring at the blank wall ahead of me. 

*Someone broke in,* my brain screamed. *Someone broke in, found Bartholomew, shoved him into a closet, put on the hoodie, and just stood in the hallway waiting for someone to come home.*

How long had they been standing there? How long had they held that perfect, terrifying stiffness? 

A horrifying thought struck me: I had just assumed they were the mannequin and *turned my back to them*.

The ragged breathing behind my left shoulder hitched, stopping completely. 

Then, I heard the faint, terrifying rustle of fabric as hands began to slowly pull themselves out of the hoodie pocket.
