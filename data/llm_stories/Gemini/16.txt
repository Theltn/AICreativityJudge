The taste of copper and ozone was fading, replaced by the bitter, metallic tang of cheap institutional coffee. 

I rubbed my temple where, forty-five minutes ago, a hollow-point bullet had made a brief, catastrophic entrance. The scarring was already gone. There wasn't even a blemish, just smooth skin and a dull, lingering ache that felt like a mild hangover.

I sat in an uncomfortably rigid chair in an interrogation room at the J. Edgar Hoover Building. The walls were grey, the table was metal, and the two-way mirror was predictably entirely unconvincing.

Agent Miller, a man whose face seemed permanently arranged in an expression of weary disbelief, walked in and dropped a manila folder onto the table. It made a loud smack.

"Are you done performing miracles in my lobby, Mr. Vance?" Miller asked, pulling out the chair opposite me and sitting heavily down.

"I tried telling the front desk," I said, leaning back and lacing my fingers together. "They said filling out the 'Anomalous Abilities' form required three forms of ID and a four-week processing period. I felt a practical demonstration was more efficient."

"You walked in, declared you couldn't be killed, put a .45 to your temple, and pulled the trigger in front of three dozen civilians and a heavily armed security detail," Miller said, his voice flat. He opened the folder. Inside were polaroids—instant, undeniable proof. One showed me crumpled on the marble floor, a vast pool of blood haloing my ruined head. The next, taken barely two minutes later, showed me sitting up, the wound knitting itself shut with terrifying speed, while the blood rapidly coagulated into dust. 

"Efficient," I offered. "And undeniable."

Miller sighed, dragging a hand across his face. "Do you have any idea the paperwork you've caused? We have forty NDAs to process before lunch." He stared at me, his eyes hard. "How?"

"I don't know," I answered honestly. "I was hit by a drunk driver in 1994. DOA. Woke up in the morgue three hours later feeling slightly chilly and incredibly thirsty. Everything had healed. The broken neck, the internal bleeding, the crushed chest cavity. Perfectly fine."

"And you've been testing this?"

"Extensively," I admitted. I rolled up my sleeve, revealing an arm that had been severed in an industrial accident and regrown over a very uncomfortable long weekend. Not a single scar remained. "Fire, drowning, blunt force trauma, exsanguination. Last year I went skydiving and intentionally failed to deploy my chute. That one hurt the most. Took a full day to reform the skeletal structure."

"You’re telling me you are biologically immortal."

"Indestructible, at least," I corrected. "I haven't aged a day since '94, so immortality seems likely."

Miller leaned forward, resting his elbows on the metal table. He didn't look terrified. He looked calculating. "Why are you here, Mr. Vance? People with your... condition usually prefer to stay off the radar. You didn't come here just skip the line at the front desk."

I met his gaze. "I'm bored, Agent Miller. Thirty years of not risking your life makes life incredibly dull. And finding a job where 'cannot be killed' is a required skill set is surprisingly difficult."

I slid the folder back toward him. 

"I want to work for you," I said. "Send me in first. Throw me into the hostage situations, the bomb defusals, the hazardous environments. I'm expendable, and I always come back. I want a badge, and I want something to do."

Miller stared at me for a long, silent minute. He looked at the polaroids of my shattered skull, then back into my perfectly healthy eyes. 

A slow, grim smile finally cracked his weary expression. 

"Do you know anything about infiltrating high-radiation zones?" he asked.
