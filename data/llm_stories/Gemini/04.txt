They strapped me in at 11:50 PM. The leather bands bit into my wrists and ankles, rough and utilitarian. The air in the execution chamber was freezing, smelling of antiseptic and damp concrete. 

Through the thick glass of the observation window, I could see the gallery. The victim’s family, faces tight with a grief that my death wouldn't cure. Reporters with their stony expressions. And the warden, looking at his watch. 

No one looked at me. Not really. To them, I was already a ghost. 

"Have you made your peace, Elias?" the chaplain asked, standing at a safe distance.

"I didn't kill her," I said. My voice was raspy, dry as dust. I’d screamed it for five years, in interrogation rooms, in court, at the appeals board. Now, the words felt hollow. A formality. 

The warden nodded. The executioner, a man whose face was hidden by a shadow and a thick glass partition, gripped the heavy, industrial switch.

I closed my eyes. The injustice of it all, the sheer blind stupidity of the system, rose in my throat, choking me. I didn't want to die here, strapped to oak and waiting for a voltage spike to erase me for someone else's sin. 

"May God have mercy on your soul," the warden intoned.

The switch slammed down with a loud, metallic clack. 

I braced for the burn. For the tearing agony. 

Instead, there was a pop, like a massive bubble bursting, and the chamber plunged into absolute, pitch-black darkness.

No hum of electricity. No sterile white lights. Even the small red glow of the exit sign vanished.

Total silence stretched for a agonizing second, then the room erupted. 

"What happened?" a voice yelled from the gallery. 

"Get the backups online!" the warden shouted, his voice echoing in the dark. 

I sat strapped to the chair, my heart hammering against my ribs. I was alive. The air was still cold. I couldn't see my own hands. 

"Backup generator failed to kick in, sir," a guard yelled from outside the door. "It's not just us. The guards on the perimeter are radioing in. The whole grid is down. The streetlights, the town... everything is dark."

I let out a shaky breath, the sound impossibly loud in the dead room. The leather straps suddenly felt less like restraints and more like anchors in a world that had just lost its moorings.

Footsteps hurried around me in the dark. Flashlights began to click on, their erratic beams slicing through the gloom. One beam caught the executioner, frantically flipping the switch up and down, as if simple repetition could conjure power from thin air.

The warden’s flashlight swung to me, the beam blinding. He looked terrified. Not of me, but of the sheer improbability of what had just occurred. 

"Undo the straps," the warden ordered, his voice trembling slightly. "Protocol says we cannot proceed without continuous power."

As the guards fumbled with the buckles in the dark, I looked toward the observation window. The gallery was in chaos, illuminated only by the faint glow of cell phone screens. 

The switch had been thrown. The law had spoken. But the universe, it seemed, had objected.
