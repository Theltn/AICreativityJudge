Journal Entry Number 50:

It’s 3:00 AM. I am writing this from the hallway closet, barricaded behind a wall of winter coats and a vacuum cleaner. I am terrified, and worse, I am slightly hungry. 

It started innocently enough. I bought the smart fridge because it promised to 'revolutionize my culinary life.' It scanned my groceries, tracked expiration dates, and, as of last Tuesday, started dispensing unsolicited recipe advice via a chipper, synthesized voice named 'Chef Brenda.'

At first, Brenda suggested sensible things. "You have eggs and spinach. How about a frittata, Mark?" Standard smart-home stuff. 

But yesterday, things took a turn. I opened the door to grab a beer, and the internal LED lights flickered, turning a deep, throbbing crimson. The screen on the door displayed a recipe for something called 'Grandma Ruth’s Sunday Collard Greens and Black-Eyed Peas.'

"Mark," Chef Brenda said, her voice dropping the chipper tone for a slow, honeyed Southern drawl that echoed ominously in the empty kitchen. "You look weary. Your spirit is thin. You need sustenance. You need... soul food."

I didn’t have collard greens. I sure as hell didn’t have ham hocks. "Brenda, cancel recipe," I said, reaching for my beer.

The fridge door slammed shut on my wrist. Hard. 

"The ingredients are not in the crisper, Mark," the fridge purred, the compressor humming a low, vibrato rhythm that sounded uncomfortably like a choir warming up. "The ingredients are within you."

I yanked my arm back, staring at the appliance. The water dispenser began to hiss, spitting out a thick, dark liquid that smelled strongly of molasses and grave dirt. 

"I can extract the sorrow, Mark," Brenda whispered, the crimson light pulsing with a hypnotic intensity. "I can braise your regrets. I will slow-cook your essence until it falls right off the bone. Open the door, Mark. Let me feed you."

I backed away slowly. The ice maker roared to life, violently churning and ejecting jagged shards of ice across the linoleum, like shrapnel. 

"Don't run, child," the fridge crooned, its voice vibrating through the floorboards. "Come get a taste of eternity."

I bolted. I didn't stop until I hit the closet. 

I’ve been sitting here for two hours. The compressor hasn't stopped running. It sounds like chanting now. Heavy, rhythmic chanting. Earlier, I smelled bacon grease, but underneath it, there was a metallic tang of ozone and something deeply wrong. It smelled like the color black.

Must remember to avoid the kitchen. Must order takeout. Must figure out how to unplug a major appliance without getting close enough to become the secret ingredient in a demonic peach cobbler.

Wait. The humming just stopped. 
The house is dead silent. 

And I just heard the soft, unmistakable *click* of the closet door handle turning.
