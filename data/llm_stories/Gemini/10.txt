Cell Block C was usually loud with the chaotic hum of a hundred caged men. But in the corner of the recreation room, at Table 4, a reverent silence held tight. 

"The dragon breathes a cone of fire," whispered Higgins, a scrawny man doing five years for embezzlement. He rolled a twenty-sided die; it clattered across the cheap plastic table, stopping beside a hastily drawn map on a napkin. "Fourteen."

"Misses my armor class," sneered 'Brick' Thompson, an imposing figure doing time for armed robbery. Brick played a Halfling Rogue with surprising delicacy. "I tuck into an evasive roll."

At the head of the table sat Marcus, the Dungeon Master. He was serving life without parole. He was a quiet, meticulous man. He adjusted his wire-rimmed glasses and looked at the group. 

"The dragon roars, frustration echoing in the cavern," Marcus narrated, his voice low. "It circles back toward the portcullis. The mechanism is old, rusted. Heavy iron bars block the exit." 

"I examine the lock," said Jenkins, a former engineer serving time for corporate sabotage, playing a Dwarven Cleric. 

"It's a complex mechanism," Marcus said, not looking at the dice, but staring directly at Jenkins. "Dual pins. Requires simultaneous pressure on the left and right tumblers. The guard... I mean, the goblin sentry on the other side changes shifts in exactly fifteen turns."

Jenkins scratched his chin, his eyes darting toward the heavy steel door at the end of the rec room. It had a dual-key system, operated by guards in the observation booth. The shifts changed at 9:00 PM. Every night.

"Simultaneous pressure," Jenkins repeated. "My dwarf has a spell for that. *Mending*. But it requires a physical component. Copper wire."

"You don't have copper wire in your inventory," Marcus noted evenly. 

"I scavenged it," Jenkins replied firmly. "From the... magical loom in the last chamber." 

Higgins, the embezzler, nodded slowly. He worked in the prison laundry room. The industrial dryers had exposed wiring behind the back panels. "I passed the copper wire to the dwarf during our rest," Higgins murmured. 

"Excellent," Marcus said. He looked at Brick. "The rogue is nearest the gate. The sentry is distracted. What do you do?"

Brick leaned forward, his massive hands steepled together. "I use my stealth. I slip out of the courtyard. I have the map of the ventilation tunnels we found in the wizard's library."

The wizard's library was the prison warden's office, where Brick had spent a week on cleaning duty, memorizing the building schematics tacked to the corkboard. 

"The tunnels are tight," Marcus said. "You'll have to navigate the primary exhaust shaft. That vent fan spins fast."

"I calculate the timing," Brick countered. "Three seconds on, one second off. I slip through during the pause." He looked at the clock on the rec room wall. It was 8:45 PM. 

"Roll for sleight of hand," Marcus said. 

Jenkins slid a small, tightly coiled piece of copper wire across the table under the guise of passing a 12-sided die to Brick. Brick palmed it effortlessly. 

"Natural twenty," Brick said, a grim smile spreading across his face. 

Marcus packed up his notes, slipping the napkin map into his pocket. "The session is over for tonight. The goblin shift is changing, and you stand before the portcullis. We resume tomorrow."

The guards called, "Lockdown!" from the observation booth. The prisoners stood up. 

"Great game, DM," Brick said, turning toward his cell block, the copper wire burning a hole in his pocket. "I can't wait to see how this campaign ends."
