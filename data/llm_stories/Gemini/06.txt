The plan was simple. Brilliant, really. 

Arthur Pendelton was not a man of grand ambitions, merely expensive tastes. He needed cash, quickly, and the 'Gateway to the Nebula' cult was his masterpiece. He invented a sprawling, nonsensical mythology about advanced energy beings residing in the Orion constellation who were returning to 'harvest' enlightened souls on the upcoming solstice.

All anyone had to do to ascend was liquidate their assets, transfer the funds to Arthur’s offshore trust to purge their 'material tether,' and ingest a special elixir precisely at midnight. 

Arthur had prepared the elixir himself: grape Kool-Aid and a hefty dose of sleeping pills. The plan was to be long gone on a flight to Belize before they woke up, groggy and utterly broke. It was a victimless crime, assuming you considered fools to be willing participants in their own fleecing.

He had gathered his flock—forty earnest, sad-eyed individuals—in a rented warehouse adorned with fairy lights and cheap crystal points. It was the night of the solstice. The funds had cleared. His suitcase was packed in the trunk of his rental car outside. 

"Brothers and sisters," Arthur intoned, draped in a white robe that smelled faintly of mothballs. He stood before a punch bowl filled with the purple elixir. "The time of harvest is upon us. We shall leave these fractured shells behind and join the Nebula."

He expected them to hesitate. To cry. To ask for a refund at the final moment. 

Instead, a profound, serene silence fell over the room. 

A frail woman named Agnes, who had signed over the deed to a three-million-dollar estate, stepped forward. Her eyes shone with a terrifying, absolute certainty. "Thank you, Archon," she whispered, taking a cup. "Thank you for showing us the way."

She drank it without a flinch. Then the man next to her. Then the next.

Arthur felt a prickle of unease. They were too eager. 

Then, something happened that Arthur absolutely had not planned for. 

As Agnes lowered her cup, a strange, humming vibration filled the air. It wasn't the low rent HVAC system. It felt like pressure building behind his eardrums. The fairy lights flickered violently, changing from warm white to a blinding, piercing blue.

Agnes gasped. Not in pain, but in shock. Her body went rigid. Slowly, impossibly, her feet lifted off the concrete floor. She was hovering. 

Arthur stared, his jaw slack. He looked at the punch bowl. Just Kool-Aid. Just pills. 

The vibration grew to a deafening roar. One by one, the congregants, having ingested the mundane concoction, began to glow. Their bodies became translucent, veins tracing like lightning beneath their skin. 

"Archon!" a middle-aged man cried out joyously as he floated toward the ceiling, his physical form beginning to disintegrate into ribbons of pure light. "It's happening! We are shedding the tethers!"

Arthur stumbled backward. He had invented the Nebula. He had made it up while hungover in a Denny's parking lot. 

The roof of the warehouse groaned and suddenly tore upward, ripped away by an unseen force. A column of blinding, violet energy descended from the night sky, engulfing the floating congregants. 

Agnes looked down at him as she dissolved into light. "Aren't you coming, Archon?" she yelled over the roar of the energy beam. 

Arthur stood rooted to the spot, terrified. He had wanted their money. He had never intended to actually give them ascension. 

The beam intensified, and then, with a concussive shockwave that blasted Arthur off his feet, it vanished. The roof was gone. The congregants were gone. The punch bowl lay shattered on the floor.

Arthur sat in the silence, millions of dollars richer, staring at the empty sky, realizing with abject horror that he had been right.
