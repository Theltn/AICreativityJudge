The wind across the salt flats was the kind of cold that stole the breath from your lungs. Elara kept her head down, joining the shuffling grey masses in line for the daily nutrient block assignment. Every citizen in Sector 4 wore identical roughspun grey, every person stared ahead with the hollow eyes of the perpetually surveilled. 

The Enforcement drones buzzed overhead, geometric shapes of sleek black metal, their red ocular sensors sweeping the crowd for anomalies. Any deviation—a raised voice, irregular movement, an unapproved gathering—was met with immediate "recalibration." 

Elara reached the rationing terminal. The automated arm extended, dropping a dense, pale grey block into her waiting hands. It was half the size it had been a month ago. Production quotas were down. The Ministry demanded sacrifices.

She tucked the block into her satchel and turned back toward the transit habs. A few paces ahead of her, a man stumbled. He was older, his grey uniform hanging off a frame stripped of all excess mass. He coughed, a dry, rattling sound, and dropped his nutrient block. 

It hit the salt flat with a dull thud. 

In any other era, someone might have helped him. Here, movement rippled through the crowd, not to assist, but to clear away. Stealing rations was a Class 1 offense, carrying a penalty of public erasure. Being near someone who dropped a ration invited suspicion. Helping them invited scrutiny. 

The man fell to his knees in the dust, his hands shaking violently as he tried to grasp the dirt-covered block. His fingers, knobby and arthritic, couldn't find a purchase. 

A drone swiveled above them, its hum increasing in pitch. Analyzing. Assessing productivity metrics. 

Elara stopped. It was dangerous to stop. The crowd flowed around them like water around a stone, ignoring the scene. 

She looked at the man, then at the drone, its red eye fixed on the struggling citizen. If he couldn't stand, if he couldn't walk to his hab, he was no longer an asset to the Ministry. The drone would summon a Sweeper unit. 

Elara didn't think. She stepped out of the flow of the crowd. She knelt beside the old man, blocking the drone's line of sight with her body. 

"Here," she whispered, her voice barely carrying over the wind. 

She reached into her satchel, broke her own meager ration block perfectly in half, and pressed one piece into his trembling hands, burying it beneath the soiled block he was trying to retrieve. 

The man looked up at her, shock widening his eyes. It was an expression Elara hadn't seen in years. It was an ancient, forgotten thing: gratitude. 

"Stand up," she hissed, grabbing his elbow and hauling him to his feet. "Walk."

The drone buzzed angrily, circling to reaqcuire its target. It scanned the old man, who was now upright, clutching his ration, though the system noted a slight discrepancy in mass. It scanned Elara. It found no violation protocol for an unacknowledged proximity interaction. 

It chirped a negative tone and flew off toward an altercation near the transport platform.

Elara didn't look back. She fell in line, her stomach cramping around the half-ration she had left. It was a foolish thing to do. It sped up her own starvation timeline. It risked the attention of the Ministry. 

But as she walked, staring at the grey backs of the citizens ahead of her, Elara realized her hands had stopped trembling. She was still starving, still trapped in the salt flats, but for the first time in her life, she didn't feel entirely diminished. In the shadow of the drones, she had chosen to be human.
