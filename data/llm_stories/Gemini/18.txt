The sound was sharp, metallic, and incredibly persistent. It sliced through the quiet hum of the coffee shop, a frantic *brrr-ring, brrr-ring* that stood out sharply against the indie-folk background music. 

Elena winced, pressing her fingertips against her temple. She sat across from Mark, holding a latte that had gone cold twenty minutes ago. 

Mark leaned over the table, concern etched on his face. "Are you alright?" he asked gently. "You've been zoning out since I got here. Headache?"

Elena squeezed her eyes shut. The ringing didn't diminish; if anything, it seemed to vibrate louder within the confines of her skull. It wasn't the dull whine of tinnitus. It was the distinct, undeniable sound of a mid-nineties rotary telephone, aggressive and demanding.

"No," she managed to say through gritted teeth. "It's ringing."

Mark frowned, looking around the cafe. "I don't hear a phone. Someone's cell?"

"No, Mark. It's... it's inside." She tapped the side of her head, right above her ear. "It's in here."

The ringing stopped abruptly. 

Elena let out a breath she hadn't realized she was holding, her shoulders sagging. "Okay. It stopped. Sorry, that was..."

But before she could finish the sentence, a voice replaced the silence. It didn't come through her ears. It bypassed her auditory system entirely, dropping directly into her consciousness like a heavy stone into a pond.

*Finally,* the voice sighed. It was a woman's voice, raspy and exhausted. *You took your sweet time answering.*

Elena gasped, gripping the edge of the table. "Did you say something?" she asked Mark, her eyes wide with panic.

"I asked if you wanted water," Mark replied, looking increasingly alarmed. "Elena, you're scaring me."

*He can't hear me, sweetheart,* the voice in her head continued, possessing a dry, sarcastic edge. *It's a designated line. Secure connection. Brain-to-brain, across a temporal distance that would make your physicist friends weep.*

Elena stood up abruptly, her chair scraping loudly against the wooden floor. She needed quiet. She needed to know she was just losing her mind, not becoming a receiver for cosmic telemarketing. 

"I need to go to the bathroom," she blurted out, turning away from Mark and practically running toward the back of the cafe. 

She locked herself in a stall, pressing her hands tightly over her ears, leaning against the cool tile wall. "Who are you?" she whispered frantically. "Stop. Please."

*I can't stop,* the voice said, entirely unperturbed by Elena's panic. *I only get a three-minute window before the probability waves collapse. Which means you need to listen to me very carefully.*

"Am I having a stroke?" Elena demanded, tears pricking her eyes. "Are you a tumor?"

The voice let out a short, harsh laugh. *I wish. A tumor you could cut out. No, Elena. I'm you. Specifically, I'm you from exactly forty-seven hours in the future.*

Elena stopped breathing. 

*I don't have time to explain the physics, mainly because neither of us understands them even when they're happening,* future-Elena said, the urgency bleeding through the sarcasm. *But you cannot get on that flight to Chicago tomorrow. Under no circumstances.*

"Why?" Elena whispered, the reality of the impossibly clear voice finally settling in, colder than ice. 

*Because if you do,* the voice answered, thick with a terror that Elena recognized intimately as her own, *in forty-seven hours, you're going to have to find a temporal anomaly and call yourself to tell you not to.*

The connection severed with a sharp, static click, leaving Elena alone in the quiet bathroom, staring at the tile, knowing her life had just irrevocably changed.
