The cure was beautiful in its simplicity. A single injection, developed by an anonymous organization calling itself the Prometheus Initiative. It didn't grant immortality—a car crash or a bullet would still end you—but it halted cellular degradation completely. It cured cancer. It reversed heart disease. It slammed the breaks on aging. 

Death by natural causes was eradicated.

The world rejoiced for exactly four years. 

By year five, the population spiked to ten billion. By year ten, it was twelve billion. 

People weren't dying, but they were still being born. The arithmetic of survival shifted from managing disease to managing sheer mass. 

At fifteen billion, the arable land ran out. Hydroponic towers scraped the smog-choked skies of every major city, but it wasn't enough. Water rationing became severe. The energy grid constantly buckled under the weight of fifteen billion perfectly healthy, perpetually active lives. 

The tipping point arrived quietly, announced in a stark, peer-reviewed paper by the Global Resource Council. Earth’s absolute maximum carrying capacity, assuming draconian resource management, was eighteen billion. Beyond that, the biosphere would undergo rapid, irreversible collapse. 

The global population was currently seventeen point five billion.

Governments panicked. Birth control was mandated, but enforcing it globally proved impossible. Wars erupted, not over ideology or territory, but over water rights and agricultural yields. The wars culled millions, but in a world without natural death, it was pulling a thimble of water from a flooded basement. 

The United Nations convened in emergency session. The solution, when it finally emerged, was birthed from abject desperation. It was called the Calculus of Existence. 

Earth could sustain exactly fourteen billion lives comfortably and sustainably. To reach and maintain that number, a system was implemented. 

It wasn't a lottery. That was deemed too cruel, too random. It was an auction. 

Every five years, the global population cap was firmly enforced. If the population exceeded the cap, the Life Exchange opened. 

Citizens were evaluated. Not on morality or potential, but on cold, hard metrics. Resource consumption versus economic and structural contribution. But the primary factor was willingness to yield. 

If you wanted a child, the state demanded a vacancy. Someone had to volunteer to take the "Sunset."

The Sunset offices opened in every major city. They were sterile, quiet places. People went in, were administered a painless, synthetic neuro-toxin, and their allotted life-quota was transferred to the state for redistribution. 

Some went willingly. The elderly, mostly, who had lived for a century and were weary of a world choked with people, tired of the endless struggle for a cup of clean water. They traded their endless lives for their grandchildren's right to exist. 

But soon, the volunteers ran out. 

The birth rate, though suppressed, continued to outpace the Sunsets. 

That was when the audits began. 

If you weren't contributing enough to the grid, if your resource footprint was deemed too heavy for your output, you received a letter. A mandatory Sunset. 

The cure for natural death hadn't saved humanity. It had simply shifted the executioner from illness and time to a cold, bureaucratic algorithm. We conquered disease, only to realize the earth itself was the final predator, and it demanded its tithe in numbers we had to provide.
