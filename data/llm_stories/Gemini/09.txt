The fog was thick over Ocean Beach, a heavy, damp blanket typical for a December morning in San Francisco. A lone jogger, an actuary named Dan, paused to catch his breath near the ruins of the Sutro Baths. The tide was surging, grey and aggressive. 

Through the mist, a shape emerged from the frothing surf. 

Dan squinted. It wasn't a seal or a piece of driftwood. It was a man. 

The figure waded out of the Pacific with slow, agonizing deliberation. He was impossibly tall, broad-shouldered, and draped in a heavy, knee-length coat that looked less like modern winter wear and more like a waterlogged, frozen cassock. He collapsed onto the wet sand, a mountain of soaked black wool and matted hair.

Dan tentatively jogged over. "Hey, buddy," he called out worriedly. "You okay? The water's freezing."

The man turned his head. His beard was massive, a ragged tangle clinging to his face, caked in salt and sand. But it was his eyes that stopped Dan dead. They were pale, luminous, burning with a frantic, hypnotic intensity that seemed entirely disconnected from his hypothermic condition. 

He coughed, spitting up seawater and what looked suspiciously like a tiny crab. 

"They... poisoned the wine," the man rasped. His accent was thick, rolling, heavily Russian. 

Dan pulled out his phone, ready to dial 911. "I'll get an ambulance. Did you fall off a boat?"

The man ignored him, pushing himself up onto his hands and knees. He swayed, glaring at the fog-shrouded horizon. "Then... the gunshots. In the courtyard. Cowards. All of them."

"Sir, just stay down," Dan pleaded. 

The man grunted, managing to haul himself to his feet. He towered over Dan, smelling of brine, ancient dust, and old copper. He looked around, taking in the cliffs, the distant, faint outline of the Golden Gate, and the actuary hovering nervously with an iPhone. 

"Where is the Neva?" the man demanded, his voice a low, booming rumble. "This is not Petrograd."

"Uh, this is San Francisco," Dan said, utterly bewildered. 

"San Francisco," the man repeated, testing the syllables on his tongue. He reached up and felt the back of his head, withdrawing a hand slick with fresh, dark blood. He looked at it, a grim smile cracking through the wet beard. "The ice was thick. It took... time to wake up."

"You really need a hospital," Dan insisted, dialing the number. 

The man reached out at lightning speed, his heavy, ice-cold hand gripping Dan's wrist. The grip was terrifyingly strong, vibrating with a strange, magnetic energy. Dan felt a sudden, overwhelming wave of calm wash over him, silencing his panic instantly. 

"I have had enough of doctors," Grigori Yefimovich Rasputin said, his pale eyes boring into Dan's. A full century of dark slumber beneath the crushing pressure of the sea had done nothing to diminish the fire within him. "Tell me, friend... who is the Tsar of this San Francisco?"
