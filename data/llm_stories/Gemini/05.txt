The heavy blast doors groaned, years of rust protesting the motion. As they parted, the air pressure hissed outward, dragging a cloud of stale, recycled oxygen into the expanse beyond. 

Elias stepped forward, his boots crunching against ground that wasn't steel grating or smoothed concrete. It was uneven. Soft. 

Behind him, a dozen children huddled together. They were pale, thin things, wearing mismatched coveralls and ocular visors meant to protect eyes adjusted only to the dim, flickering glare of subterranean lumens. They ranged from age six to twelve. None of them had ever been above Level 3. None had ever seen the sky.

"Alright," Elias said, his voice sounding strange, unconfined by metal corridors. "You can take them off."

Slowly, hesitantly, twelve small pairs of hands reached up and pulled down the dark visors. 

Gasps rippled through the group. 

Elias looked out at the wasteland. To him, it was a graveyard. The sky was an unforgiving canopy of uniform, bruised purple-grey, choked with particulates that hadn't settled since the war. The landscape was a jagged ruin of twisted rebar, shattered concrete towers, and vast, dead plains of ash. There was no sun, just a diffuse, sickly glow that cast no shadows. It was a world that had been broken and left to rot.

He braced himself for their terror, for the tears.

"Look!" cried a tiny girl named Elara, pointing a trembling finger toward a monolithic chunk of a collapsed skyscraper. A colony of pale, bioluminescent fungus had taken root against its base, pulsing with a faint turquoise rhythm. "It's glowing. Like the emergency lights, but... pretty."

A boy next to her knelt down. He plunged his hands into a drift of grey ash. To Elias, it meant death, the remains of an incinerated city. The boy scooped it up, letting it sift through his fingers. "It's like dust, but everywhere. Like... like snow!" The boy had only read about snow in the archives. He scooped another handful and threw it in the air, laughing as it rained down around him.

The children scattered, drawn by the utter novelty of disaster. To them, the massive craters weren't scars of orbital bombardment; they were grand, mysterious valleys. The jagged, rusted husks of military vehicles were climbing frames of epic proportions. 

A heavy, toxic wind moaned across the plain, bringing with it the scent of sulfur and ozone. Elias closed his eyes, fighting a wave of despair. 

"Elias, what is that?" 

He opened his eyes. Elara was looking straight up. The cloud cover had thinned fractionally in one spot, revealing not the sun, but a small, jagged patch of a lighter, metallic grey. 

"Is that the ceiling?" she asked, her voice hushed in awe. "It's so high. How do they change the bulbs?"

Elias looked at these kids, born into a choked, sunless tomb, finding wonder in the apocalypse. They didn’t see the end of the world. They just saw a bigger, wilder version of home.

"They don't have to," Elias whispered, a strange, tight feeling in his chest. "It changes itself."
