The headache was blinding, the heat oppressive, and the smell of roasting lamb overwhelmingly intense. I blinked against the harsh sunlight, sitting up in the dust. My clothes felt wrong—heavy linen instead of my usual jeans and t-shirt. 

I was sitting in the middle of a bustling, open-air market. The language being shouted over the bleating of livestock and the clatter of bronze coins wasn't English. It wasn't anything modern. It possessed the rhythmic, harsh cadence of Latin. 

I looked down at my lap. Resting there was a massive, leather-bound tome, roughly the size of a microwave. 

My head throbbed as I traced the gilded letters on the cover: *Wikipedia: The Complete Printed Edition - 2024*. 

It was impossible. The logistics alone of printing the entire English Wikipedia were absurd, yet here it was, thousands of gossamer-thin pages crammed into a single, impossibly dense volume. I flipped it open randomly. Page 42,918: *Differential Equations*. Page 112,404: *History of the Roman Empire*. 

Ah. That explained the centurions patrolling the market edge. 

I scrambled to the index, my hands shaking. *Augustus*. Reign began 27 BC. 

I was in Rome. It was 27 BC, the dawn of the Empire, and I held the sum total of human knowledge in my lap. 

Panic seized me, swiftly followed by a terrifying, intoxicating realization. I wasn't just a time traveler; I was a god waiting to happen. 

I needed a plan. I couldn't just walk up to Augustus Caesar and hand him a schematic for a steam engine. I'd be crucified for witchcraft before the sun set. I had to be subtle. I had to be indispensable. 

I flipped to the section on basic sanitation and germ theory. It was dense text, but the principles were simple enough to explain to a bronze-age physician without sounding insane. Boil water. Wash hands. I could cure dysentery. 

I turned to metallurgy. The Bessemer process was too advanced, but basic improvements to iron smelting? Easily achievable. 

I spent the first few weeks living like a beggar, painstakingly translating key articles into crude, broken Latin. I started small. I found a struggling potter and showed him a design for a hotter, more efficient kiln based on thermodynamics principles I barely understood but could copy perfectly from the diagrams. Within a month, he was the wealthiest artisan in the district, and I was his 'silent partner.'

Then, I moved to agriculture. Crop rotation. Advanced irrigation based on Roman aqueduct foundations but optimized using fluid dynamics. The yields were staggering.

Word spread of the strange foreigner with the 'Book of the Gods.' My Latin improved. My wealth grew. I didn't introduce gunpowder—that was opening a box I couldn't close. I introduced penicillin. I introduced basic algebra to the architects. 

By the time I was granted an audience with a senator closely allied with Augustus, five years had passed. Romans were living longer. Structures were being built taller and safer. 

He looked at me, a man who possessed no royal blood but wielded influence greater than any general. 

"They say you are a prophet, Elias," the senator said, pouring a cup of wine. 

I placed my hand on the massive leather tome, worn smooth by constant use. "Not a prophet, Senator. Just a very well-read historian from a place that hasn't happened yet."

I opened the book to the article on the Library of Alexandria. "Now, let me tell you why we need to build a fireproof archive."
