Elias Crane never believed in anything—not gods, not fate, not the afterlife. But he did believe in money. And fear, he discovered, was the most profitable currency in the world.

The idea started as a joke.

"A suicide cult," he’d said to his friend Rowan over cheap whiskey. "But one where no one actually dies. They just think they will. Then I take their money and disappear."

Rowan laughed. Elias didn’t.

Within a month, he had a name: **The Ascendants**.

Within two, he had followers.

Within six, he had a fortune.

He preached transcendence, rebirth, liberation from earthly suffering. He wore white robes and spoke in a soft, trembling voice that made people lean in. He promised them a glorious death that would lead to a glorious beyond.

Of course, there was no beyond. Just a warehouse with incense, dim lights, and a trapdoor leading to a padded room where the "departed" would wake up confused but unharmed.

Elias would greet them with a serene smile.

"You have transcended. Your old life is gone. Your possessions now belong to the Ascendants."

Most accepted it. They were desperate, lonely, searching for meaning. He gave them a new identity and kept their money.

It worked.

Until it didn’t.

One night, during a ceremony, Elias noticed something strange. The followers weren’t chanting the words he taught them. Their voices were lower, unified, rhythmic in a way he had never heard.

Rowan leaned toward him. "Did you change the script?"

"No," Elias whispered.

The chanting grew louder.

**“Ascend. Ascend. Ascend.”**

The air thickened. The candles flickered violently. A cold wind swept through the warehouse though the doors were shut.

Elias forced a smile. "My children, tonight you will—"

"We know," said a woman in the front row.

Her eyes were wrong. Too wide. Too bright.

"We are ready," said another.

"Ready for what?" Elias asked.

The crowd turned toward him in perfect unison.

**“To ascend.”**

Rowan grabbed his arm. "Elias… something’s wrong."

The followers stepped forward, moving as one. Their faces were serene. Too serene.

"Listen," Elias said, raising his hands. "There is no real ascension. It’s symbolic. A metaphor. You don’t actually—"

"We know," the woman repeated.

Then she smiled.

"But you promised us death. And we want it." 

Elias felt the blood drain from his face. "No. No, that’s not—"

The chanting resumed, louder, vibrating the walls.

**“ASCEND. ASCEND. ASCEND.”**

Rowan backed away. "Elias, we need to run."

But the followers closed in, forming a circle around them.

Their eyes glowed in the candlelight.

Their smiles were too wide.

And Elias realized, far too late, that he had created something he could no longer control.

Something that no longer needed him.

Something that wanted exactly what he had promised.
