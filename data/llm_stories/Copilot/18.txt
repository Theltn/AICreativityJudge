The train was nearly empty when the stranger sat across from me.

Not next to me.

Not diagonally.

Directly across, knees almost touching mine, like he’d been waiting for that exact seat.

He wore an old-fashioned suit—charcoal gray, perfectly pressed, not a wrinkle in sight. His hair was slicked back. His shoes gleamed. Everything about him looked polished.

Except his eyes.

They were tired.

"Long day?" he asked.

I blinked. "Uh… yeah."

He nodded knowingly. "They always are."

The train lurched forward. Fluorescent lights flickered overhead.

"Do you ride this line often?" he asked.

"Sometimes."

"Good. Routine is important. It keeps the world predictable. Stable."

I gave a polite smile. "I guess so."

He leaned forward.

"But tonight won’t be predictable."

A chill crawled up my spine. "What do you mean?"

He didn’t answer. Instead, he reached into his coat and pulled out a pocket watch.

A beautiful one—silver, engraved, ticking softly.

He opened it.

The hands spun wildly.

"We’re almost out of time," he murmured.

I swallowed. "For what?"

He looked up, and for the first time, I noticed something strange.

His reflection wasn’t in the train window.

Mine was.

The empty seats behind me were.

But not him.

"You’re not—" I began.

"Human?" he finished. "No. Not anymore."

The train lights dimmed. The car rattled violently.

"What’s happening?" I shouted.

He closed the pocket watch.

Everything stopped.

The train froze mid-motion. The lights hung in place, flickering but unmoving. Even the dust in the air stopped drifting.

Time had halted.

The stranger stood.

"Listen carefully," he said. "In thirty-seven seconds, this train will crash. Everyone on board will die."

My heart pounded. "But you stopped time. You can fix it."

He shook his head. "I can’t change what’s coming. I can only choose who walks away."

He extended a hand.

"Come with me."

I stared at him. "Why me?"

His expression softened.

"Because you’re the only one who sees me."

I hesitated.

Behind him, the frozen passengers sat unaware, suspended in their final moments.

"If I go with you," I said, "what happens to them?"

He lowered his hand.

"They stay."

"And if I stay?"

He sighed.

"Then you stay."

The train groaned, reality straining against the pause.

"Choose," he said.

I looked at his hand.

At the frozen world.

At the people who didn’t know they were already gone.

And I made my choice.
