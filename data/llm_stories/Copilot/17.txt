The message arrived at 3:12 a.m.

Not by phone.

Not by email.

It appeared on my bedroom wall.

Glowing letters, written in a trembling white light:

**DON’T GO TO WORK TOMORROW.**

I sat up so fast I nearly fell out of bed. The letters flickered, then burned brighter.

"Hello?" I whispered.

The message changed.

**PLEASE.**

My heart hammered. "Who are you?"

The letters dissolved, then reformed.

**A FRIEND.**

"What kind of friend writes on my wall in the middle of the night?"

The light dimmed, as if embarrassed.

**THE ONLY ONE WHO CAN.**

I rubbed my eyes. "This is a dream."

**NO.**

"Then what are you?"

The letters stretched, reshaped, and finally settled into two words:

**YOUR REFLECTION.**

I froze.

Slowly, I turned toward the mirror across the room.

My reflection didn’t move.

Not at first.

Then it lifted its hand.

I didn’t.

I scrambled backward. "What do you want?"

The reflection pressed its palm against the glass.

The wall behind me lit up again.

**IF YOU GO TO WORK, YOU DIE.**

My mouth went dry. "How?"

**ACCIDENT.**

"What kind of accident?"

**DOES IT MATTER?**

Fair point.

"Why warn me?"

The reflection’s expression softened—my face, but sadder.

**BECAUSE I DIDN’T GET A WARNING.**

A chill crawled up my spine. "What does that mean?"

The lights flickered. The reflection leaned closer to the glass.

**I’M YOU. FROM THE OTHER SIDE.**

"The other side of what?"

**THE DAY YOU’RE ABOUT TO LIVE.**

I swallowed hard. "So… in your version, I died?"

**YES.**

"And you’re trying to stop it?"

**YES.**

"Why?"

The reflection hesitated.

Then the wall lit up one final time.

**BECAUSE I DON’T WANT TO BE ALONE.**

The letters faded.

The mirror went still.

I didn’t go to work.

And I never saw the reflection move again.

But sometimes, late at night, I catch it watching me.

Not with malice.

Not with fear.

Just… relief.
