I always thought the characters were just drawings.

Ink. Color. Pixels.

Nothing more.

That was before I took the job at Starfall Animation Studios.

My first week was normal enough—coffee runs, storyboard scanning, late nights fixing continuity errors. But on Friday, my supervisor, Lorna, handed me a badge I’d never seen before.

"Basement access," she said. "You’re on cleanup duty tonight."

"Cleanup?" I asked.

"You’ll see."

The elevator descended past the labeled floors—Animation, Rendering, Sound, Archives—then continued downward into unmarked territory. When the doors opened, a cold draft hit me.

The hallway lights flickered.

At the end was a steel door with a keypad.

My badge unlocked it.

Inside was a cavernous room filled with glass cells. Each one glowed faintly, like a lantern. And inside each cell… something moved.

I stepped closer.

My breath caught.

In the first cell stood Ember, the fire spirit from *Flamebound*. Her hair flickered like a candle. Her eyes were terrified.

She pressed her hands to the glass. "Please," she whispered. "Let me out."

I stumbled back.

In the next cell, Captain Orion from *Star Rangers* paced like a caged tiger, his holographic armor glitching at the edges.

"Another handler?" he growled. "Or are you here to watch us suffer?"

"I—I don’t understand," I said.

A voice behind me replied, "They’re not suffering. They’re performing."

Lorna.

She stepped into the room, arms crossed.

"You didn’t think animation was *drawn*, did you?" she asked. "That’s so twentieth century. We discovered a way to pull fictional constructs into physical form. They act. We record. Everyone wins."

"They’re alive," I said.

"Of course they’re alive. That’s what makes them valuable."

Ember slammed her fists against the glass. "We are prisoners!"

Lorna sighed. "They get dramatic. Occupational hazard."

I stared at her. "This is wrong."

"It’s profitable," she corrected.

She handed me a tablet. "Your job is to keep them stable. Feed them energy. Monitor their emotional output. If they get too distressed, their forms destabilize. And we can’t have that."

I looked at the characters—heroes, villains, sidekicks—each one trapped, each one real.

"I won’t do this," I said.

Lorna smiled thinly. "You already signed the contract. And if you quit… well, we always need new characters."

My blood ran cold.

Behind her, Captain Orion met my eyes.

He mouthed one word.

**Help.**

I tightened my grip on the tablet.

If the studio wanted me to manage these characters…

Then I would.

Just not the way they expected.
