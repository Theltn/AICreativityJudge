The guards thought it was just a game.

That was the beauty of it.

Every Thursday night, in Cell Block C, the prisoners gathered around a battered wooden table. Dice clattered. Pencils scratched. Laughter—real laughter—echoed through the concrete halls.

To the warden, it was harmless recreation.

To us, it was the blueprint for freedom.

I was the Dungeon Master. Not because I was the best storyteller, but because I was the only one who could keep the metaphors straight. Every monster was a guard. Every trap was a camera. Every dungeon was a hallway we needed to cross.

And every quest was a step toward escape.

Tonight’s session was the most important.

“Alright,” I said, pretending to consult my notes. “Your party stands before the Iron Gate. Two ogres guard it. They’re slow, but strong. What do you do?”

Rico leaned forward. “We distract them. Same as we planned.”

He wasn’t talking about ogres.

He was talking about Officers Briggs and Mallory—the two guards who patrolled the west corridor.

“Roll for deception,” I said.

He rolled a 17.

The table erupted in quiet cheers.

“Success,” I said. “The ogres lumber away, confused.”

Big Tom grinned. “Then we slip through the gate.”

Translation: we move during the shift change.

I nodded. “You enter a long hallway lit by torches. At the end is a locked door.”

Jenna tapped her pencil. “What kind of lock?”

I met her eyes. “A simple one. Old. Rusted.”

She smiled. She was our lockpick.

“Roll for sleight of hand.”

She rolled a 19.

“Success,” I said. “The door opens.”

The others exchanged glances. We were close—both in the game and in reality.

But then the worst possible thing happened.

A guard approached.

Officer Mallory stopped at the bars. “What’s all this?”

“D&D night,” I said casually. “Want to join?”

He snorted. “Nerds.”

But he didn’t leave.

He watched.

Listened.

Too long.

I had to throw him off.

“In the next room,” I said loudly, “you find a treasure chest.”

The others stiffened. That wasn’t part of the plan.

Mallory smirked. “Treasure, huh?”

“Yep,” I said. “But it’s trapped. Deadly poison. Roll to disarm.”

Big Tom rolled. A 3.

I slammed my hand on the table. “Critical failure!”

The group groaned.

Mallory laughed. “You idiots.”

But he walked away.

As soon as he was gone, Rico whispered, “What was that?”

“A warning,” I said. “We wait until tomorrow. Too risky tonight.”

They nodded.

The game continued.

The plan lived.

And somewhere between fantasy and reality, freedom waited—just behind the next locked door.
