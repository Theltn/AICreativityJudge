The first sign that something was wrong with the house was the laughter.

Not ghostly laughter. Not evil laughter. Not even unsettling laughter.

Just… children’s laughter.

Which would’ve been fine, except I lived alone.

I’d bought the place cheap—too cheap—because the previous owner had moved out suddenly. The realtor said it was due to "personal reasons." I should’ve known that was code for "the house is weird."

The first night, I heard giggling in the hallway. Light, playful, like kids chasing each other.

I opened the door.

Nothing.

The second night, I found toys on the stairs. Wooden blocks. A tiny red truck. A doll missing one shoe.

I didn’t own toys.

By the third night, I was ready to call a priest, an exorcist, or maybe a demolition crew.

But then I saw them.

Three children. Transparent, glowing faintly blue, floating a few inches above the floor. They looked at me with wide, curious eyes.

"Hi!" the smallest one chirped.

I froze. "Uh… hello?"

"We live here!" she said proudly.

"We do not live here," the oldest corrected. "We haunt here. There’s a difference."

The middle child floated closer. "Do you want to play?"

"Play?" I repeated.

"Yeah!" the youngest said. "The last guy didn’t like playing. He screamed a lot."

"We tried to make him feel welcome," the oldest added. "But adults are weird."

I stared at them. "You’re… ghosts."

"Yep!" they said in unison.

"And you want to… play?"

"We get bored," the middle one said. "Being dead is dull."

I rubbed my temples. "Okay. What kind of games?"

Their faces lit up.

"Hide and seek!" the youngest shouted.

"Tag!" said the middle.

"Or we can show you the secret room," the oldest said casually.

I blinked. "Secret room?"

He pointed toward the basement. "Behind the wall. The one with the scratch marks."

My stomach dropped. "Scratch marks?"

"Yeah," he said. "From the man who lived here before us. He didn’t like the room either."

The youngest tugged my sleeve. "We can show you!"

I swallowed hard. "Maybe later."

They looked disappointed.

"Can we at least play tag?" the middle one asked.

I sighed. "Fine. One game."

They cheered.

And that’s how I ended up sprinting through my own house at midnight, being chased by three giggling ghost children who could walk through walls.

I was out of breath. They weren’t.

When the game ended, they floated in a happy little cluster.

"You’re fun," the youngest said.

"You can stay," the oldest added.

"Uh… thanks?"

They drifted toward the ceiling.

"Goodnight!" they called.

I collapsed onto the couch, exhausted.

The house was haunted.

But at least the ghosts were friendly.

Probably.
