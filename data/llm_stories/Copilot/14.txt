The first rule of the library was simple: **Do not read aloud.**

I thought it was a joke.

A dusty sign taped to the front desk, written in fading marker. A bored librarian who didn’t even look up when I walked in. Rows of ancient books stacked to the ceiling, their spines cracked and titles worn away.

It felt like the kind of place where rules were more suggestion than law.

I was wrong.

I came looking for a genealogy record—something about my great‑grandfather’s immigration papers. The librarian pointed me toward the back, where the oldest books lived.

The air grew colder as I walked. The lights buzzed overhead. The shelves creaked like they were breathing.

I found the book easily enough. A thick leather tome with no title.

When I opened it, dust spiraled into the air like smoke.

The text inside wasn’t English. Or any language I recognized. The letters twisted on the page, rearranging themselves when I blinked.

I leaned closer.

And without thinking, I whispered the first line.

The floor trembled.

A low groan echoed through the stacks.

I snapped the book shut.

The groaning stopped.

My heart hammered. I told myself it was just old building noises. Settling wood. Faulty wiring.

But then I heard footsteps.

Slow.

Heavy.

Coming from between the shelves.

"Hello?" I called.

The footsteps stopped.

Then a voice—dry, papery, like pages rubbing together—whispered back.

**"Read it again."**

I backed away. "Who’s there?"

The lights flickered.

A shadow stretched across the floor, long and thin, bending around corners in ways shadows shouldn’t.

**"Read it again,"** the voice repeated.

I shook my head. "No."

The shadow twitched.

**"You opened the book. You spoke the words. You woke us."**

"Us?" I whispered.

The shelves shuddered. Books fell to the floor, pages fluttering like wings.

From the darkness between the stacks, shapes emerged—tall, skeletal figures made of torn paper and ink, their eyes hollow circles of black.

They moved toward me.

**"Read,"** they hissed. **"Finish what you began."**

I bolted.

The creatures followed, their limbs rustling like dead leaves. The library warped around me—aisles stretching, doors vanishing, lights dimming.

I sprinted toward the front desk.

The librarian didn’t look up.

"Help!" I shouted.

She sighed, closed her book, and finally met my eyes.

"You read aloud, didn’t you?"

"Yes!" I gasped. "They’re coming!"

She stood, joints cracking like old bindings. "Then you’d better run faster."

Behind me, the paper creatures shrieked.

The librarian stepped aside.

"Rule number one exists for a reason," she said.

I didn’t look back.

I just ran.
