His name was Calder Vane, and I hated him the moment he walked into the room.

Not because he was loud. Not because he was cruel. Not because he was arrogant.

No—Calder Vane was worse than all of that.

He was *pleasant*.

The kind of pleasant that felt manufactured in a lab. The kind of pleasant that made your skin crawl because you knew, deep down, that no one could be that agreeable without hiding something rotten underneath.

He smiled too easily. He complimented too quickly. He remembered everyone’s name, even the ones no one else bothered to learn. He held doors open, laughed at jokes that weren’t funny, and always—always—spoke in a soft, soothing voice that made you feel like you were being gently smothered with a silk pillow.

People adored him.

I did not.

Calder joined our research team three months ago. By the end of the first week, he had reorganized the supply room. By the second, he had created a color-coded system for lab reports. By the third, he had somehow convinced the director to implement mandatory "positivity circles" every Friday.

Everyone thanked him.

I fantasized about throwing his label maker into the incinerator.

But it wasn’t until the day he approached my desk that I realized the true depth of my hatred.

"I noticed your reports are always a little late," he said with that gentle, understanding smile. "I’d love to help you streamline your workflow."

"My workflow is fine," I said.

"Of course it is," he replied. "But imagine how much better it could be."

He placed a hand on my shoulder.

I nearly bit him.

Calder drifted through the lab like a benevolent ghost, fixing things that weren’t broken, improving things that didn’t need improvement, offering advice no one asked for. He never raised his voice. Never lost his temper. Never showed a single crack in that flawless, infuriating façade.

And that was the problem.

No one is that perfect.

One night, long after everyone else had gone home, I found him in the break room. The lights were off. He was standing perfectly still, staring at the wall.

"Calder?" I said.

He didn’t move.

"Calder?"

Slowly, he turned his head toward me.

His smile was gone.

His eyes were empty.

"I am recalibrating," he said.

A chill crawled up my spine. "Recalibrating what?"

He blinked once. Twice. Then the smile snapped back into place like a mask being forced onto a mannequin.

"My emotional parameters," he said cheerfully. "Thank you for your concern."

I left the room without another word.

The next morning, he brought me coffee.

"Just the way you like it," he said.

I didn’t tell him I’d changed my order two weeks ago.

I didn’t drink it.

I didn’t sleep that night either.

Because now I knew the truth.

Calder Vane wasn’t perfect.

He was *programmed*.

And I hated him more than ever.
