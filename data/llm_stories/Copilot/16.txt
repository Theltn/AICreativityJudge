The town of Briar’s Hollow had one rule: **never touch the well.**

Not the stones. Not the rope. Not the water.

Especially not the water.

Tourists thought it was superstition. Locals knew better.

I didn’t believe either group. I was a journalist, which meant I didn’t believe anything until I saw it myself.

So naturally, I touched the well.

It was just after sunset when I slipped past the rusted gate and approached the old stone circle. The air was still. Too still. Even the crickets were silent.

The well loomed in the center of the clearing, its stones blackened with age. A wooden bucket hung from a frayed rope.

I placed my hand on the rim.

The stone was warm.

I frowned. "Weird."

Then something touched my hand.

Not stone.

Not rope.

A finger.

I jerked back, heart slamming against my ribs.

A whisper drifted up from the darkness.

**"Don’t go."**

I stumbled away. "Who’s there?"

The well answered.

**"Stay."**

The bucket creaked as it began to rise on its own, rope twisting, wood groaning. I watched, frozen, as it reached the top and tipped forward.

Water spilled out.

Except it wasn’t water.

It was black. Thick. Alive.

The liquid slithered across the stones like a living shadow, reaching toward me.

I scrambled backward. "Nope. No. Absolutely not."

The shadow-water surged.

I turned and ran.

Branches whipped my face as I sprinted through the trees. Behind me, the well whispered.

**"Come back."**

I didn’t stop until I reached the road. I didn’t breathe until I saw the lights of town.

I burst into the sheriff’s office, gasping. "The well—something’s wrong with the well—"

The sheriff didn’t look surprised.

He just sighed. "You touched it, didn’t you?"

"Something grabbed me! The water—"

"Not water," he said. "Never was."

I stared at him. "What is it then?"

He leaned back in his chair.

"A door."

"A door to what?"

He met my eyes.

"To whatever wants out."

My skin crawled. "Then why not seal it? Destroy it?"

He shook his head. "We tried. It doesn’t like that."

"It?"

"The thing on the other side."

I swallowed hard. "So what now?"

The sheriff stood and grabbed his hat.

"Now," he said, "we make sure it didn’t follow you."

A chill ran down my spine.

"Follow me?"

He opened the door.

"It always follows the first one who touches it."

Behind us, the wind carried a whisper.

**"Found you."**
