They stood at the edge of the broken overpass, five children wrapped in patchwork coats, staring at the horizon with wide, unblinking eyes. I watched them from a distance, my breath fogging in the cold air.

The sky above us was a permanent bruise—purple, gray, and sickly green where the clouds thinned. No sun. Not for years. Not since the Veil.

But the children didn’t know any different.

“Look at that,” little Mara whispered, pointing at a rusted-out car half-buried in ash. “It’s beautiful.”

Beautiful. A word that had nearly vanished from adult vocabulary.

I approached slowly. “What do you see?”

They turned to me, faces smudged with soot, eyes bright with a kind of wonder I hadn’t seen since before the world collapsed.

“It’s shiny,” Mara said. “Like treasure.”

“It’s just metal,” I said gently.

“Metal that survived,” said Jax, the oldest. “That means it’s strong.”

I swallowed hard. They had grown up in a world where survival itself was a marvel.

Jax hopped down the slope toward the car, boots crunching on frost. He ran his hand along the door, leaving streaks in the grime. “Imagine how fast it went,” he said. “Imagine riding inside it.”

The others gasped as if he’d described flying.

I remembered traffic jams, honking horns, the annoyance of being late for work. These kids saw a miracle.

“Can we open it?” Mara asked.

“Careful,” I warned.

But Jax had already tugged the handle. The door groaned open, releasing a puff of stale air.

Inside, the seats were torn, springs exposed. A stuffed toy lay on the floor—one ear missing, one button eye dangling.

Mara picked it up reverently. “A creature,” she breathed.

“It’s a toy,” I said.

“A toy,” she repeated, as if tasting the word. “People used to make things just to make others happy?”

“Yes,” I said softly. “They did.”

The children exchanged looks of awe.

Jax climbed into the driver’s seat and gripped the wheel. “What did this do?”

“It steered the car.”

He twisted it experimentally. “Feels powerful.”

I smiled despite myself. “It was.”

The wind howled through the skeletal remains of the city. The children didn’t flinch. They were used to it.

“Tell us more,” Mara said. “About the world before.”

I hesitated. The world before had been loud, bright, careless. We had wasted everything because we believed it would last forever.

But these children—born into darkness—looked at ruins and saw wonders.

So I sat with them in the cold, inside the shell of a car that would never run again, and I told them about sunlight.

How it felt on your skin. How it made colors bloom. How it warmed everything it touched.

They listened with rapt attention, eyes shining.

“Will we ever see it?” Mara asked.

I didn’t know. No one did.

But looking at their faces—full of hope in a world that had none—I found myself saying, “Maybe.”

And for the first time in years, I almost believed it.
