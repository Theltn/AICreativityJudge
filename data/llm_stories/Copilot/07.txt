The drones hummed overhead, scanning the street for anyone foolish enough to be outside after curfew. I kept my head down, clutching the ration bag to my chest. The city was a maze of cracked concrete and flickering propaganda screens, each one barking the same message:

**OBEY. PRODUCE. ENDURE.**

I tried to ignore them. I tried to ignore everything.

Then I saw her.

A little girl, no older than eight, sitting on the curb with her knees pulled to her chest. Her clothes were threadbare. Her hair hung in tangled ropes. And she was crying—quietly, the way children learn to cry when loudness is dangerous.

I hesitated. Helping her meant stopping. Stopping meant being seen. Being seen meant punishment.

I should have walked away.

Instead, I crouched beside her. "Hey. Are you hurt?"

She shook her head without looking up. "They took my passcard. I can’t go home."

My stomach tightened. Without a passcard, she’d be flagged as a vagrant. Vagrants disappeared.

"Who took it?" I asked.

"The enforcers," she whispered. "They said I was loitering. I wasn’t. I was waiting for my mom."

A drone drifted closer. I felt its red sensor sweep across my back.

I made a choice.

I reached into my coat and pulled out my own passcard. "Here. Take mine."

Her eyes widened. "But… you need it."

"I’ll manage."

She hesitated, then took it with trembling hands. "Thank you."

"Go straight home," I said. "Don’t stop for anything."

She nodded and ran, small feet slapping against the pavement.

The drone pivoted toward me.

"Citizen," it droned, "present identification."

I stood slowly. "I… don’t have it."

The drone whirred, processing. "Violation detected. You will be detained for questioning."

I raised my hands.

Then, from the shadows, a voice called out.

"He’s with me."

A woman stepped forward—the girl’s mother. Her face was pale, but her eyes were fierce.

She held up the passcard. "He dropped this."

The drone scanned it. "Identity confirmed. No violation."

It drifted away.

The woman exhaled shakily. "You saved my daughter."

"I just did what anyone would do."

She shook her head. "No. Not anymore."

The girl peeked out from behind her mother and gave me a small, shy wave.

For the first time in a long time, something warm flickered in my chest.

A small act. A tiny rebellion. But in a world built on cruelty, kindness was the most dangerous thing of all.
