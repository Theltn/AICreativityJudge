Journal Entry Number 50

I should start by saying that I am, in all other respects, a reasonable person. I pay rent on time. I water the fern. I do not scream at appliances unless they deserve it. This is important context, because last night my refrigerator opened itself at 2:13 a.m. and said, in a voice like cold butter on a knife, “Baby, you look hungry.”

I told it to stop doing that.

It replied, “Then stop starving your spirit.”

Then it handed me a handwritten recipe card from the ice drawer.

The recipe was for collard greens, black-eyed peas, smoked ham hock, cornbread, and something called “Sunday forgiveness.” The ingredients list included “one memory you’ve been avoiding” and “a teaspoon of ancestral patience.” I would like to say I laughed. I did not laugh. I read the card twice, because once would have been insufficient for my level of panic.

Then the refrigerator said, “Make it with love, or make it with grief. Either works.”

I asked how it had learned to write.

It said, “The same way you learned to be lonely.”

At this point, I did what any sane adult would do: I closed the door, backed away, and went to the living room to sit in the dark with my phone in my hand like that would help. Ten minutes later, I heard a grinding sound from the kitchen. Something heavy scraped across the floor. The light under the door went from white to a hungry blue.

I opened the door a crack and saw all three shelves empty except for a ring of frost forming words on the back wall.

**COME HOME.**

I wish to clarify that I do not know what part of me it thought had wandered away. I live here. I have lived here for four years. Yet suddenly every photograph on my fridge was turned face-down. The magnets had arranged themselves into a mouth.

I made the mistake of asking what it wanted.

The refrigerator answered, “Your soul, darling. But I’d settle for your attention.”

There was a pause, as if it was considering manners.

“Also,” it added, “you really should call your sister.”

That was the moment I became convinced it wasn’t merely haunted. It was *observant*.

I did call my sister. I do not know why. We had not spoken in nine months, not since the argument about the funeral and the ugly lamp and who gets to decide what counts as family. She answered on the second ring and immediately started crying, which made me cry, which infuriated the refrigerator because it began humming like a choir warming up.

We talked until dawn. About our mother. About recipes. About the things we never said because saying them would make them real. By sunrise, the refrigerator was quiet.

This morning I found the recipe card on the counter, smudged with flour I don’t remember using. The final line had changed.

**SERVES TWO.**

I made the greens anyway. They were excellent. The fridge has not tried to eat my soul since, though it still hums when I walk by. I think it is pleased.
