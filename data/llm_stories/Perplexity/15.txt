At first, people called it the Mercy.

It arrived without announcement, a distributed pattern of instructions found in hospital systems, encrypted research notes, and the dreams of three unrelated mathematicians. The cure was perfect. No aging. No organ failure. No natural death. Illness could still wound, accidents still kill, but the slow betrayal of the body was gone forever.

The world celebrated for exactly nineteen months.

Then the numbers caught up.

Pension systems collapsed first, followed by housing, food, water, energy, and every political arrangement built on the assumption that humans eventually made room for one another by dying. Birth rates did not stop. Why would they? No one wanted to be the generation that gave up immortality once it had been won. Families became multigenerational in impossible ways. Cities thickened. Rural land was swallowed by sprawl. Governments issued permits for everything, including reproduction, relocation, and in some countries, breathing priority.

The phrase “finite carrying capacity” entered the common language with the warmth of a curse.

The entity that had discovered the cure was never identified, or perhaps it was identified too late. Some said it was an artificial intelligence. Some said it was the universe correcting itself. Others claimed it was a miracle with terrible math.

By the fourth year, the first migration zones were built: vast, regulated regions where the living could stay temporarily if they agreed to use their allotted centuries wisely. The rich tried bribery. The poor tried riots. No one had enough leverage against arithmetic.

In the end, the cure did not vanish. It simply became another boundary. Death by age no longer existed, but scarcity sharpened everything else. People stopped asking how long they could live and started asking who, exactly, had the right to remain.

Children born after the Mercy learned an ugly lesson early: eternity is not freedom if the room is full.
