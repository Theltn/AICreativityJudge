The guards thought the prisoners’ weekly game was harmless.

That was their first mistake.

The second was assuming that a table full of men with sharpened pencils, hand-drawn maps, and an enthusiasm for imaginary elves could not possibly be plotting anything useful. In Cell Block C, the game began every Thursday after count. A cardboard screen stood between the players and the cameras. Dice clicked. Voices rose and fell in the cheerful cadences of fantasy.

In truth, the campaign was a message system.

“Your rogue finds a hidden passage under the chapel,” said Marcus, who served as dungeon master because he had the best memory and the steadiest nerves. He did not look up from his notebook. “It’s narrow, but passable.”

That meant: the chapel corridor was unguarded during shift change.

“I check for traps,” said Eli.

“Two pressure plates, one on the left, one on the right.”

The players nodded as if this were perfectly ordinary.

“Can we get through by moonrise?” asked Tomas.

“Only if the wizard can clear the gatehouse,” Marcus replied.

That was the signal to the man in the far corner, who played a wizard with a suspiciously detailed knowledge of ventilation shafts. He adjusted his glasses and said, “I cast *mending* on the broken door hinge.”

The whole table hummed approvingly.

Over months, they mapped the prison in fragments disguised as adventures. The infirmary became a cursed tower. The laundry chute was a goblin tunnel. The maintenance closet behind the chapel was referred to, with great solemnity, as the dragon’s hoard. Guards who listened in heard only talk of spells, quests, and heroic sacrifice. They never learned that “finding the ring” meant stealing a master key, or that “fighting the ogre” meant timing a riot in the yard.

One night, a new prisoner was assigned to the table. The guards had warned him not to cause trouble. He sat down, rolled the dice, and listened.

After an hour he looked up and said, too casually, “If the north wall is weak, why not make the break there?”

Silence.

Marcus met his eyes. The others kept their faces neutral.

Then he smiled. “Because the north wall is guarded by the lich.”

The new prisoner stared, then slowly grinned back. He had understood.

By spring, the escape plan was no longer a theory. It was a campaign nearing its final session. The date had been chosen. The routes were marked. The roles were assigned. When the final dice rolled, Cell Block C would not be a prison anymore.
