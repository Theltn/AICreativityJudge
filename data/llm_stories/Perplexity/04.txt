They had dressed him in white, as if cleanliness could persuade the state it had not made a mistake.

Cal Mercer sat strapped into the chair while the room watched him with the fixed, professional solemnity reserved for irreversible things. The governor’s pardon had not come. The appeal had failed. The newspapers had already moved on to weather and sports. Only the old librarian from Cal’s neighborhood had written once, in a shaky hand, saying she believed him. That letter was folded inside his shoe now, hidden beneath the leather strap.

“Any final words?” the warden asked.

Cal swallowed. “I didn’t kill her.”

No one in the room moved. They had heard it before.

The executioner nodded toward the switch. He looked tired, not cruel, which was somehow worse.

When the lever came down, the world vanished.

No flash. No crack. No violent surge.

Just darkness.

For one stunned second, no one understood. The backup generators failed to answer. The fluorescent lights deadened into black glass. Somewhere outside, alarms began to wail and then cut off mid-note. The room became a held breath.

Then a voice spoke from the dark.

“Finally.”

Cal strained against the straps. “Who’s there?”

A scrape sounded near the wall, then another. Not footsteps exactly. More like something moving in a long-hidden space between the building and the world.

The emergency lights flared red, weak and uneven. In their glow, the execution chamber looked wrong. The corners seemed deeper than they should have been. The air had gained a wet, mineral smell.

The executioner backed away from the switch. “What did you do?”

Cal laughed once, sharp and hysterical. “You’re asking me?”

A shape emerged from the far wall, tall and bent, with arms too long and a face full of patient shadows. Another followed it, and another. They were not human, though they had clearly learned enough of humans to imitate their fear.

“We’ve been waiting under the grid,” the first one said. “You opened the door.”

The warden stumbled back. “What are you?”

“Power customers,” said the thing. “And hungry.”

The room dissolved into chaos. Doors jammed. Radios sputtered dead. Somewhere in the prison, hundreds of inmates began shouting as every light in the facility went out at once. The creatures moved like spilled ink, flowing from conduit to conduit, from shadow to shadow, and the guards’ bullets passed through them as though into water.

Cal, still strapped to the chair, found himself laughing again, but now it was the sound of a man who had gone past terror and found something else there.

The first creature leaned close. Its voice was oddly kind.

“You were not supposed to die here,” it said. “That was the error.”

“Can you undo this?” Cal asked.

The thing tilted its head. “We can do better.”

It touched the chair, and the metal unraveled into darkness.
