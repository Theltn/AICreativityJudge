The mannequin was named Bernard because one of the roommates had insisted that all household objects deserved dignity.

It started as a joke. On Monday, Maya posed Bernard holding a frying pan. On Tuesday, Dev gave him sunglasses and a hoodie. On Wednesday, Leah placed him in the hallway with a paperback and one bent knee like a disaffected art student. By Friday, Bernard had become part of the apartment’s ecosystem, an immobile witness to dishwashing wars and passive-aggressive notes on the fridge.

So when Ellis came home late from work, dropped his keys, and noticed a person standing near the kitchen sink, he did not panic.

He paused only long enough to think, *That’s an excellent Bernard pose.*

The figure had one hand on the counter and one shoulder tilted toward the fridge. The posture was so natural, so blandly decorative, that Ellis barely registered the fact the head had turned. He kicked off his shoes, tossed his bag on the couch, and said, “Nice. Very lifelike.”

The person in the kitchen stayed still.

Ellis walked around them to get a glass of water, frowning at the way everyone had upped their game. “Seriously, who did this? It’s creepy.”

Then he noticed the cabinet door hanging open.

The sink was wet. There were muddy prints near the balcony. And the air had that sharp, wrong smell of someone who did not belong.

Slowly, Ellis looked back at the figure by the sink.

Not Bernard.

A stranger. Real. Breathing.

The stranger smiled with the frozen caution of someone who had been caught pretending to be furniture.

Ellis stared.

The stranger stared back.

From the living room came Maya’s voice. “Did someone move Bernard again?”

Ellis did not answer immediately, because the truth had not fully reached language yet. Then the stranger raised one finger, as if asking for discretion.

Ellis set down his glass very carefully and said, with awful calm, “We have a situation.”
