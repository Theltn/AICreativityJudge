The bus began shrinking before anyone noticed.

At first it was only the seats. Then the aisle. Then the windows, which swelled with the impossible pressure of somewhere too large to fit inside a classroom. Carlos was the first to say something. “Uh, Mrs. Frizzle?”

She turned from the driver’s seat with her usual dazzling grin, but there was a tension at the corner of her eyes that had never been there before.

“Don’t worry, class,” she said. “We’re just taking a tiny detour.”

That was when the sky outside the windows changed color.

Instead of clouds, there was a swirling red darkness dotted with structures that looked far too much like bones.

Phoebe leaned forward. “Where are we?”

Mrs. Frizzle’s necklace rattled as the bus lurched. “Inside a living thing.”

Arnold made the sound he made whenever the universe failed to follow safety protocols. “Inside what living thing?”

Mrs. Frizzle stared at the dashboard. “I may have misread the label.”

The bus shuddered. Something enormous and wet passed against the outside hull, and the students screamed. Timmy’s backpack floated. The entire vehicle vibrated with a deep, organ-like throb.

Liz, the lizard, vanished under a seat.

“We were supposed to study the circulatory system,” Mrs. Frizzle said brightly, with the too-fast cheer of a person who absolutely understood this was catastrophic. “But it seems we have entered the wrong organism.”

“Wrong organism?!” Arnold yelled.

Through the windshield, a shape moved in the crimson haze: a massive eye the size of a house, opening slowly, glistening and aware.

Ms. Frizzle looked at it and went pale.

The eye blinked.

Then, with a sound like a mountain speaking, the creature inside which they were trapped said, “Who are you?”

Nobody answered.

The bus lights flickered. The emergency sign above the door flipped to **NO EXIT**.

Then, from somewhere deep in the creature’s body, another voice answered—small, frightened, and unmistakably human.

“Please,” it said. “Don’t let it find us.”
