By the time the first human was discovered in District Nine, the alarm had already been filed, stamped, and routed to the proper office.

This was one of the many reasons the Kingdom of Morrowvale had thrived for centuries under undead stewardship. The dead were punctual. They did not panic. They preserved everything they touched, including the roads, the libraries, and the peace. Their children learned arithmetic from bone tablets. Their markets sold moss, salt, polished metal, and carefully labeled memories. The harvest never came late because no one in government remembered how to lie about weather.

So when Scout Venn reported flesh-pricked footprints on the eastern marsh path, the city responded with admirable speed.

Within an hour, Inspector Halrix, who had once been a judge and now served as a specialist in contagion law, stood in front of the captured human. It was a small thing, barely adult, with a torn coat and eyes bright with exhaustion and terror.

The humans had been thought extinct for three hundred years.

“Name,” Halrix said.

The creature flinched. “Mara.”

“Origin?”

“East of the river. Maybe farther. I left when the others did.”

“Others,” Halrix repeated, making a note. “How many?”

“Enough.”

That was not helpful, but the undead had built a civilization on patience. They quarantined Mara in a white room lined with ash-salt. Physicians examined her pulse, her heat, her breathing, all with an unsettling curiosity.

The city’s first reaction was fear, though it behaved in a very orderly manner. Humans, the old histories warned, brought hunger that was too alive to name. They planted too fast. They moved too fast. They multiplied with the reckless certainty of fire.

By evening, the Ministry of Civic Continuity had issued guidance. Citizens were told not to approach humans without gloves, to report any sightings immediately, and not to accept invitations to “startup communities,” which was apparently how outbreaks were spreading.

Because there were outbreaks. Everywhere.

Humans emerged in sewers, in abandoned barns, in old crypts, along forgotten shorelines. They were not an army. They were a rumor turning solid. And the strangest part was that they seemed just as frightened of the undead.

A week later, Halrix visited the quarantine wing and found Mara drawing on the wall with charcoal.

“What is that?” he asked.

“A map.”

“Of where?”

“The place we thought we were going.”

He studied the lines. “You still think there are more of your kind?”

Mara smiled sadly. “Do you still think we’re the disease?”

Halrix had no answer ready for that. The city had expected infection, but what it got was inconvenience, grief, and negotiation.

The first human baby born in Morrowvale was delivered in a hospital where the staff had no heartbeat and unlimited patience. The child cried, and every undead physician in the room froze, not in fear but in awe. The sound was terrible. It was also beautiful.

The outbreak became a census. The census became a treaty. The treaty became a new ministry.

Years later, the old maps would be revised. New districts would rise where the humans preferred sunlit gardens and the undead preferred cool stone. Markets would sell both coffee and formaldehyde tea. Children would learn that thriving and surviving were not the same thing, and that civilization was sometimes just the art of making room.
