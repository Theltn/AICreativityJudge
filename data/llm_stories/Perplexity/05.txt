The children from the undercity had never seen the sky.

They knew its shape only from stories told by elders who had once been young enough to believe in weather. The sun, they were told, was a fire that no one was allowed to touch. Clouds were myth. Birds were impossible. Their world was concrete tunnels, lamp glow, ration lines, and the endless silver hum of the shield overhead. It protected them from the ashfall above, or so the adults said, though most adults had long ago stopped explaining what exactly had been ruined.

When the convoy arrived from the surface, the children lined up behind the chain-link fence and stared.

The visitors wore clean boots and color in their sleeves. They brought fruit, fresh bread, paper books, and a toy that chirped when squeezed. The undercity children watched in disbelief as one woman opened a box and pulled out a small lamp powered by sunlight.

“Light,” whispered a little boy.

“It’s only a reading lamp,” the woman said kindly.

To him it looked holy.

Everything here must be amazing in their eyes, thought Talia, the convoy’s medic, as the children watched the bread break apart in warm steam. One girl stared at the orange peel as if it were a jewel. Another touched the sleeve of Talia’s jacket and then pulled her hand back in wonder, astonished that fabric could be soft.

Talia’s stomach tightened. She had brought supplies because the administration had called this a routine relief drop. But nothing was routine when people had been made to inherit scarcity so thoroughly they mistook ordinary life for magic.

The children followed the convoy through the lower district, past hydroponic vats and water filters and a mural someone had painted of a blue sky no one trusted. They asked questions in a rapid, cautious way, as if hope might be punished for speaking too loudly.

“Is grass real?”

“Yes.”

“Is it sharp?”

“Sometimes.”

“Do people live near the sun?”

“No. Not directly.”

“Why not?”

Talia smiled sadly. “Because it’s hot.”

They stared, offended by how simple the answer was.

At the end of the day, one of the smallest children—thin, solemn, no more than six—stood in front of the convoy and held out a pebble polished smooth by years of pocketing. “Will you take this upstairs?” he asked.

“Of course.”

“Will it come back different?”

“Yes.”

He considered that, then nodded as if this confirmed a theory. “Everything does.”

That night, after the convoy left, Talia looked back at the undercity and understood the terrible thing about hope: it did not arrive grandly. It arrived in small, almost invisible portions, and made everything else seem missing. The children below would keep imagining the surface as a miracle until someone taught them otherwise. But maybe that was how all worlds began—by being larger in the mind than they were in the hand.
