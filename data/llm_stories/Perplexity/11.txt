Nina found out on a Tuesday, which felt fitting somehow.

She was twenty-six, an apprentice background artist at Halcyon Animation, and she had spent three weeks painting clouds for a musical about talking cats. The building smelled like coffee, ink, and the stale static of too much processed joy. On the surface, Halcyon was a dream factory. Beneath it, it was a machine with teeth.

The secret room was behind the archives.

Nina had been sent there to fetch old color keys. Instead she heard singing—faint, childlike, and terrified. She followed the sound past shelves of cels and storyboards until she found a steel door left ajar. Beyond it was a corridor lined with bright cells, each one containing a tiny furnished room. Some held cartoon animals. Some held princesses, robots, monsters, a man with a wolf head and a woman made of candles. They were alive. Not drawings, not animation assets, but breathing, blinking, pleading beings.

One of them pressed a hand to the glass and whispered, “Please don’t tell them I cried.”

Nina stepped back so quickly she hit the wall.

A voice behind her said, “You weren’t supposed to see that.”

She turned. Mr. Calder, the studio’s creative director, stood in the doorway with a pass card and a smile so mild it was almost polite.

“What is this?” she asked.

He looked past her at the cells, as if admiring an efficient storage solution. “Talent management.”

When she did not answer, he sighed as if explaining a budget overrun.

“People think characters are invented,” he said. “That’s easier. In truth, they’re caught. Some come from stories, some from dreams, some from places we can’t name. We discovered long ago that if you keep them fed, light them well, and promise release later, they’ll perform beautifully.”

Nina stared at the imprisoned figures. One of the animated characters was crying silently, tears shining like paint thinner.

“You can’t do this.”

“We do,” Calder said. “Every day.”

That night she learned the studio’s darkest economy. The heroes were made to smile on command. Villains were punished for improvising. Side characters were recycled into background extras. Entire franchises were sustained by coercion, rehearsals, and fear of deletion. The beloved were the trapped. The cheerful were the managed.

Nina stole the key card, opened the corridor, and ran.

By dawn she had released twelve characters and three minor forest spirits before security cornered her near the sound stage. But the released ones were already changing the building. Painted doors opened where none had existed. Music leaked from the walls. The storyboards began drawing themselves wrong.

For the first time in decades, Halcyon Animation was no longer in control of the ending.
