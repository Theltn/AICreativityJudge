Elias Vane had the particular charm of men who believed their own appetites were a form of intelligence.

He was handsome in the deliberate way of an expensive knife: polished, narrow, and made to cut. He spoke softly in meetings and loudly in private, always with the implication that he could have been kinder, if only others were worth the effort. He never apologized without adding context, never gave without ledger, never loved without ownership.

Children disliked him immediately, which was one of his many talents. They could sense he considered them future audiences. He forgot waiters’ names on purpose. He read tragedy as a kind of decoration. If a friend succeeded, he congratulated them with the faint, poisoned smile of a man who had already begun to imagine their failure.

He collected virtues the way other people collected watches: for display, not use. Integrity when it benefitted him. Generosity when observed. Courage only in stories where the danger was symbolic and someone else had already bled.

At work, he took credit with immaculate calm. At home, he kept books on empathy beside books on strategy, not because he wished to understand people, but because he enjoyed knowing exactly where they broke. He was never loud, never obvious, never the sort of villain who could be confronted in a room full of witnesses. He was worse: plausible.

Elias would remember your birthday and weaponize the fact. He would lend money, then remind you of it during your lowest moment. He would comfort a grieving friend with one hand while checking his phone with the other. If caught in a lie, he would act wounded, making you apologize for noticing.

The worst part was that he was not even empty. He was full—full of precision, resentment, vanity, and a vicious little talent for making every room colder when he entered it.
