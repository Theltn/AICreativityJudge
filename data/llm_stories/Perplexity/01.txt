They named the ship *Ark Horizon* because no one could agree whether it was a beginning or an ending. After six generations of speeches, simulations, accidents, and one disastrous casserole famine, it finally crossed the heliopause and entered the black beyond. The crew gathered in the observation bay expecting wonder, silence, maybe a cathedral of stars.

Instead, a pale flash bloomed ahead of them.

A vessel slid out of nowhere, long and narrow as a needle, with lights along its hull that pulsed in patient colors. It matched speed, then projected a rectangle of text across the *Horizon’s* front glass.

**WELCOME TO THE OUTER LINE. PLEASE PRESENT TRAVEL PURPOSE, BIOSANITARY STATUS, AND DECLARATION OF INTENT.**

Captain Imani stared at the message. “That’s not possible.”

Her engineer let out a laugh that cracked halfway into panic. “Sir, we are four hundred billion kilometers from home.”

The alien ship emitted a gentle chime, as if acknowledging a delay.

“Open channel,” Imani said.

A face appeared on the screen, though face was too simple a word for something built from shifting geometry and soft light. It had two dark lenses, a narrow slit of a mouth, and a badge pinned to what might have been a uniform. The badge read: **GALACTIC PERIMETER AUTHORITY**.

“First vessel from Sol,” it said in fluent, irritated English. “About time.”

Imani recovered first. “You’re telling me space has a border?”

The officer blinked once. “Of course it does. What did you think, that civilization was an infinite hallway with no doors? That’s a charming amateur theory.”

“We were never informed.”

“That is how border violations usually begin.”

The crew began to crowd the room, some furious, some delighted, some already trying to record everything. The officer’s expression remained bureaucratically calm.

“Your system is unregistered,” it continued. “Your species is not yet classified. Your ship’s propulsion is technically admissible, but your emissions are sloppy. Before you proceed, you’ll need inspection.”

“Inspection for what?”

The alien tapped the badge. “Contraband ideas. Dangerous microbes. Unlicensed expansionism. Also any evidence you are about to become everyone else’s problem.”

A murmur rippled through the crew.

Imani folded her arms. “We’re explorers.”

“Everyone says that at the frontier.”

The vessel extended a long sensor arm and brushed the *Horizon’s* hull. Somewhere below deck, a machine shut itself off with an offended whine. The officer looked up.

“Hm. You brought poetry.”

“It’s a colony ship,” said Imani. “We brought libraries.”

The officer seemed genuinely unsettled for the first time. “Libraries are worse.”

Before anyone could ask why, another light appeared beyond them, then another—dozens, maybe hundreds of ships forming a vast invisible line in the dark. The border officer straightened.

“Listen carefully,” it said. “You are not the first to leave home. You are only the first to arrive here with your optimism intact.”

Imani leaned toward the screen. “And if we don’t pass inspection?”

The officer’s badge flashed red.

“Then,” it said, “you will be redirected.”

“Where?”

The alien looked past her, into the ship, into the people packed shoulder to shoulder with all their inherited hopes.

“To the place,” it replied, “where all first questions go when they are answered honestly.”

The message cut out.

The stars outside dimmed as the border opened.
