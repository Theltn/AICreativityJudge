“Are you alright?”

“No,” said Mara, pressing both palms against the sides of her head, “it’s ringing.”

Her friend Jonah frowned. “Your ears?”

“My *brain*.”

That was bad enough on its own, but Mara was standing in the middle of the grocery store’s produce aisle with a shopping cart full of lemons and a look of genuine terror. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. A toddler stared at her from near the apples.

Jonah lowered his voice. “What do you mean, ringing?”

Mara stared straight ahead. “Like a bell under water. Like somebody is trying to call me from very far away.”

He was about to say that she should sit down when the store lights dimmed.

Every screen in the building—checkout displays, security monitors, the digital ad above frozen foods—flared at once with static. Customers turned. The ringing in Mara’s face seemed to grow louder, though no one else could hear it. She swayed.

“Hey,” Jonah said, steadying her elbow. “Talk to me.”

Mara swallowed hard. “I think it started this morning.”

“With what?”

“The dreams. The city under the lake. The woman with no eyes. She kept saying my name like she knew me.”

A metal shelf rattled. Somewhere in the back room, glass shattered. The intercom crackled and died.

Jonah looked around. People were backing away now, uneasy, giving them space as if panic were contagious. Maybe it was.

Then Mara dropped her hand.

For one silent second, the ringing stopped. Her face went blank with shock.

And in the quiet that followed, both of them heard it: a single, clear voice speaking directly into the air above the lettuce.

“We found you.”
