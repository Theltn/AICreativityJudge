The first thing I learned about 27 BC is that nobody here knows what a copyright warning is.

The second thing I learned is that my backpack, apparently, contains the most powerful object in the Roman world: a printed, spiral-bound copy of Wikipedia.

I arrived somewhere near a muddy road outside a city that I later identified, through anxious page-flipping, as Rome. A man in a wool cloak stared at my shoes. I stared at his sandals. Then I did the least useful and most modern thing possible: I opened the encyclopedia at random.

“Octavian,” I whispered.

The page was a catastrophe of future knowledge. Rise to power. Senate reform. Pax Romana. Augustus. Death. Everything, all at once, trapped in my hands like a dangerous rumor.

I closed the book immediately, because if anyone saw me reading the next hundred years before they happened, I might alter history just by looking guilty.

By day three, I had learned that footnotes are useless when no one recognizes your source. By day five, I had traded a paragraph about aqueducts for shelter and a loaf of bread. By day eight, I was regretting every entry related to plumbing, mathematics, and electricity. Ancient Rome is not impressed by a person who can describe smartphones if that person cannot also make a decent knife.

Still, the book was useful.

I used it to avoid getting poisoned, to identify useful herbs, to win a minor argument about volcanic ash, and once to convince a suspicious magistrate that I was an eccentric philosopher from the future, which was more plausible than the truth. I did not reveal the whole thing. I valued being alive too much.

The most difficult part was restraint. Every page contained a temptation. I could predict storms, wars, emperors, betrayals. I could become rich, or powerful, or worshipped. Instead I spent most of my time trying to preserve the physical integrity of the only paper object left in history with too much information and not enough context.

On the final page I read that the year would eventually be remembered as one of transition.

That felt generous.

I closed the book, looked at the city of smoke and marble and ambition, and decided that if I was going to be trapped in antiquity with an encyclopedia, I would at least try to be a person worth footnoting.
