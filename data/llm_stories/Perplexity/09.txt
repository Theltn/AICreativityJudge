By the time the first report came in, the city had already decided it was a prank.

Then the second report arrived, then the third, and then a video so clear that nobody could dismiss it: a bearded man in a soaked black coat walking out of the Pacific at dawn, flanked by gulls and stunned tourists, as if he had simply taken a wrong turn through a century.

It was December 29, and the Bay had the color of tarnished steel.

At Fisherman’s Wharf, crowds gathered behind police tape while the man climbed the steps with an absurd, regal slowness. Water streamed from his sleeves. His boots left dark prints on the concrete. A woman near the front whispered, “That’s not possible.”

The man looked at her with deep, exhausted eyes.

“In my experience,” he said in Russian-accented English, “that has never stopped anything.”

The name spread before the officials could contain it. Rasputin. Grigori Rasputin. The supposed mad monk. The murdered, buried, mythologized relic of a crumbling empire.

He accepted no explanation, only a coat, tea, and a state-issued room where he sat beneath fluorescent lights like a man awaiting the next betrayal. He answered questions with terrible patience.

“How did you survive?”

“Poorly.”

“Where were you?”

“Underwater.”

“For how long?”

He thought for a moment. “Long enough to be disappointed.”

The historians argued. The intelligence agencies panicked. The religious broadcasters called him an omen. The city, being San Francisco, treated the whole thing with a mixture of awe, irritation, and speculative entrepreneurship. Within twelve hours, there were t-shirts.

Rasputin requested only one thing: to see the bay at night.

When the escort took him to the water, he stood very still, looking at the lights reflected in the black surface. For a moment he seemed less like a ghost and more like a man who had once been loud enough to disturb history and had spent a century regretting the sound.

A reporter asked from behind the barrier, “Why come here?”

Rasputin smiled, and for the first time it looked almost kind.

“Because,” he said, “the sea has finished speaking to me.”
