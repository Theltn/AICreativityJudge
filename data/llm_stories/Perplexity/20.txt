For fourteen nights, Daniel had slept in the left side of the bed out of habit and remorse.

On the fifteenth night, the whispers began.

At first they were only the edges of sound, threads brushed against the ear. He told himself it was the pipes, the wind, the old house settling in its bones. Then the words became clear.

“Open the closet.”

Daniel sat up, heart hammering. The bedroom was washed in moonlight and shadow. His wife’s dressing table stood untouched. Her robe still hung on the back of the door because he had been unable to remove it. He listened.

“Open it,” the whisper said again.

He swung his feet to the floor and stood, too quickly, with the room tilting around him. Every instinct told him not to move, but fear is a stronger engine than guilt. He crossed the room and pulled the closet door open.

Nothing.

Coats. Boxes. Shoes lined neatly below.

Then the whisper came again, not from the closet but from behind him.

“Wrong one.”

Daniel turned.

The voice was coming from the hallway, from the dark crack beneath the laundry room door, from the space where his wife had locked away things she no longer wanted to discuss. He walked toward it with the stale, crawling dread of a man approaching his own judgment.

Inside the laundry room, the washer was running though he had not turned it on.

The sound was steady, almost gentle.

He opened the lid.

Water churned inside. Something pale moved below the surface.

Daniel recoiled so hard he struck the wall. The whispers multiplied, layered now with his wife’s voice and other voices he did not recognize. They were not angry. That was somehow worse. They sounded patient.

He staggered backward, his hand catching the shelf above the sink. A box fell, spilling photographs across the floor. The top picture landed face up.

It was him, standing over the bed two weeks ago, lit by the yellow light of the lamp, his expression hollow with what he had done.

On the back of the photo, written in his wife’s neat hand, were six words:

**Now you hear what we heard.**
