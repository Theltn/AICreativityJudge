The agent across the table had the expression of a man trying very hard to remain employed.

“You understand,” he said, “that most people do not demonstrate immortality by attempting homicide in a federal parking lot.”

I wiped a smear of blood from my eyebrow and nodded. “It seemed efficient.”

The interview room was beige, fluorescent, and too cold. A tape recorder sat between us like a judgment. Behind the one-way mirror, silhouettes moved in and out of view. Somewhere outside, someone was still cleaning up the mess I had made to prove a point nobody wanted to hear.

Agent Bell flipped a page on his notepad. “Name.”

I told him.

“Date of birth?”

I gave him the date.

He looked at me for a second longer than necessary. “You do realize that if you are lying, this becomes a much simpler case.”

“I’m not lying.”

He sighed. “Walk me through it again.”

So I did. The illness that never progressed. The broken arm that healed in the wrong order and then corrected itself. The fall from the bridge. The knife. The fire. The bullet in the head, which had been intended as definitive but turned into a very loud misunderstanding.

When I finished, the room was quiet except for the recorder’s faint whir.

Bell leaned back. “And why come to us?”

“Because I’m tired,” I said. “And because someone is trying to kill me in ways that are getting increasingly creative.”

That got his attention.

“Who?”

“I don’t know yet.”

He studied me like a puzzle with missing pieces. “You expect the Bureau to help a man who just resurrected on federal property.”

“I expect,” I said, “the Bureau to be curious.”

He almost smiled.

Then the intercom crackled overhead, and a voice came through: “Agent Bell. We have a situation.”

Bell closed his eyes briefly. “What now?”

A pause.

“Sir,” the voice said, “the body in evidence locker four is gone.”

Bell stared at me.

I stared back.

Somewhere in the ventilation shaft, something scraped, as if listening.
