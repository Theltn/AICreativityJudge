Sir Edric had expected fire, screams, maybe a skull pile arranged with theatrical malice.

Instead he found a nursery.

The cavern was vast and warm, lit by lava flows behind iron bars and strings of glowing crystals hung from the ceiling like a strange celestial diagram. Gold lay in heaps along the walls, yes, but it had been pushed aside to make room for blankets, carved toys, and a painted rocking horse missing one eye. At the center of it all sat the dragon, enormous and scarred, curled protectively around a small stone table.

At the table sat Edric’s daughter.

And beside her, perched on a cushion, was a young dragon no bigger than a hound, with bright scales and soot on its snout. The two of them were holding hands.

Edric nearly dropped his sword.

“Lina?” he said.

His daughter looked up, startled but not frightened. “Papa!”

The adult dragon raised its head slowly. Its eyes were ancient, amber, and tired in the way of creatures who had seen too many knights come to simplify their lives.

“You are early,” it rumbled.

Edric recovered enough to lift his blade halfway. “What have you done with my child?”

Lina frowned. “Nothing. I came here on purpose.”

The young dragon gave a small, nervous puff of smoke and hid behind her shoulder.

Edric stared. “On purpose?”

Lina scrambled down from the stool and ran toward him, stopping only when the dragon’s shadow shifted behind her. “You never listen when I say I don’t want to marry Lord Pell,” she said. “And you said the dragon was a monster, so I wanted to meet it.”

The dragon made a sound that might have been embarrassment. “She arrived with bread.”

Edric blinked.

“She asked questions,” the dragon continued. “Then my hatchling liked her, and then she fed him, and then she stayed.”

The little dragon squeezed Lina’s fingers tighter. She squeezed back.

Edric’s sword suddenly felt absurd in his hand.

“I came to slay you,” he said, because there seemed no other honorable thing to admit.

The dragon’s lip twitched. “Yes. I had gathered that from the helmet.”

Silence settled between them, not hostile now but stunned.

Lina looked from her father to the dragon and back. “Can we not do the killing part?”
