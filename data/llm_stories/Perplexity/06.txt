Rafe Vale had always been good at finding the seam between loneliness and faith.

He started with a rented warehouse, a borrowed suit, and a story about transcendence. He told people the world was a trap, that pain was a tax on the living, that he had discovered a private passage out. He called it the Meridian Circle because it sounded ancient and expensive. The first members were desperate, then devoted, then eager to recruit others. Donations arrived in envelopes, then transfers, then boxes of cash wrapped in grocery bags.

Rafe was not a believer. He was a manager.

The Circle sold release. It sold dignity. It sold the promise that your last act could be meaningful if you signed the right papers and transferred your assets before the final ceremony. Rafe took ten percent, then twenty, then a private cut hidden in shell accounts. He bought a lake house he never visited and a watch he did not deserve.

The problem with lying to people about death is that some of them start treating it like a customer service experience.

At first, members simply disappeared, leaving notes and empty apartments. Then they began arriving early, demanding better instruction, brighter robes, more “purity infrastructure.” One woman wanted a livestream. A teenager asked for branded ash urns. Someone proposed a tiered membership model.

Rafe tried to shut it down. He told the inner circle the project had matured, that they should pause recruitment, that the next phase required discretion. But the cult had developed a theology without him. They believed he was not the founder but the herald, and that the true exit required sacrifice.

Then the lawsuits began.

Families came forward. Journalists appeared. Police investigated. The Circle’s finances unraveled into fraud, coercion, and missing persons. Rafe, who had always imagined himself as the spider, discovered too late that he had built a web beneath a cliff.

The final ceremony was supposed to be private. Instead, three hundred followers gathered in a former theater, lit by candles and phone screens. Rafe stood backstage, sweating through his shirt, while the congregation chanted his name like a prayer and a threat.

He had intended to flee through the loading dock. The doors were locked.

When he stepped onto the stage, the room fell silent with terrible expectation.

A young woman from the front row lifted her face. “Show us,” she said.

Rafe looked at the crowd—half true believers, half people too frightened to leave—and for the first time in his life felt the shape of a consequence larger than money.

He opened his mouth to lie again.

Nothing came out.

Somewhere in the audience, someone began laughing. Then another. The sound spread, not amused but disillusioned, and then angry. The atmosphere cracked. The illusion broke.

By dawn, the Meridian Circle was gone. Rafe was arrested trying to board a train under another name. He would spend years insisting he had merely created a community, not a death machine. No one believed him.
