The mannequin's name was Denise, which tells you everything you need to know about my roommates.

We bought her at a thrift warehouse on a Saturday after too much cold brew and not enough judgment. She was headless, one hand missing, and wore a permanent stance of mild disappointment. Kyle said she had "presence." Jenna said our apartment needed a unifying concept. By midnight Denise was standing in the hallway in sunglasses and one of my hoodies.

It became a routine.

Every day someone posed her somewhere new. At the fridge holding a grocery list. On the toilet with a newspaper. In my room, once, looming beside the bed in a bathrobe so that I woke at 3 a.m. and nearly departed this earth.

The rules evolved naturally: Denise could not be placed inside the shower, in the freezer, or hanging from the ceiling after the incident with the command strips. Otherwise, anything was fair game.

After a month, we stopped reacting the way normal people would. That was the real problem.

You can habituate to anything if it stands quietly enough.

On Thursday I came home late from work carrying Thai takeout and the kind of headache that makes the edges of furniture feel personal. The apartment was dim except for the lamp by the couch. Someone had left Denise by the front window in a long coat and baseball cap, facing the street.

"Very funny," I muttered, setting my keys down.

She didn't move.

Of course she didn't move.

I passed within two feet of her on the way to the kitchen. The coat smelled faintly wrong, though I only registered that later. Sweat, maybe. Wet concrete. The sort of smell you assign to city weather when you don't feel like thinking harder.

I ate standing over the sink. I texted Jenna about rent. I listened to Kyle laughing with someone through the wall of his room. At one point I looked up and saw Denise reflected in the microwave glass, still by the window, and felt the usual flicker of resentment at living in a home curated by raccoons.

Then Kyle came out for water, saw the figure, and went completely white.

"Who is that?"

"Denise," I said.

"Denise doesn't have ears."

We both turned.

The figure by the window flinched.

Not much. Just enough. A living person's involuntary adjustment after holding still too long.

The takeout carton slid from my hand.

Kyle made a sound I had previously only heard from kettles. The figure bolted, knocking over the lamp, and ran for the hall. Up close he was just a guy in a stolen coat and cap, maybe thirty, gaunt and desperate, face slick with panic.

He must have slipped in through the fire escape while we were out. He must have heard us come home and frozen in place, realizing the apartment already contained a human-shaped object no one questioned.

He made it three steps before Jenna, returning from the laundry room with two detergent jugs, hit him in the knee hard enough to fold him.

The police later called it one of the strangest burglary reports they'd ever taken.

I still live with Kyle and Jenna. Denise still lives with us too, now upgraded with a replacement head from Etsy and a tiny police whistle around her neck.

We don't pose her near the window anymore.

None of us can handle the possibility that one day she'll move and we'll assume she belongs there.
