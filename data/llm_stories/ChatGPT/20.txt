Two weeks after he killed his wife, Martin began waking at 3:17 in the morning.

At first he thought it was guilt, though guilt was not the word he used with himself. Martin preferred practical terms. Stress. Nerves. Residual adrenaline. Guilt implied a moral framework he found inconvenient now that Elena was under the crawl space wrapped in a tarp and six bags of garden lime.

The house had settled badly since then.

Every old place makes sounds at night, but these had changed in quality. The pipes clicked like knuckles. The floorboards breathed. Sometimes, half asleep, Martin thought he heard movement under the bedroom floor directly beneath his side of the bed.

Still, he managed.

He answered police questions with injured patience. He told Elena's sister they had argued and Elena had gone to stay elsewhere. He washed the trunk liner twice. He went back to work. Routine, he had learned, was the best disguise.

Then the whispers started.

Not in dreams. In the room.

He woke at 3:17 to a sound like dry leaves brushing together. He lay still, pulse banging, and listened. The whisper came again, too soft to parse, threading through the dark from somewhere near the bedroom door.

"Hello?" he called, and hated himself for it.

Silence.

He turned on the lamp. Empty room. Closed door. His own face in the wardrobe mirror, gray and sweaty and older than it had been two weeks earlier.

The next night the whisper returned, closer.

By the fourth night it had acquired shape. Syllables. Breath. A woman's cadence speaking from the hallway just beyond sight.

Martin got out of bed.

The house was cold enough to raise gooseflesh on his arms. He moved through the hall with one hand against the wall, listening. The whispers drifted ahead of him, retreating room by room as if leading him. Kitchen. Dining room. Laundry. Always just beyond understanding.

He checked windows, locks, the back door. Nothing.

In the kitchen he stood over the sink and told himself exactly what he would have told anyone else: auditory stress response. Lack of sleep. The mind punishing itself with tricks.

Then the garbage disposal turned on by itself.

Martin stumbled backward. The switch was down. The motor roared anyway, grinding emptiness with furious metallic hunger. From the black mouth of the drain came the whisper, now unmistakable.

Martin.

He shut it off at the breaker and fled to the living room, where the whispers simply resumed from the heating vent.

Martin.

He dropped to his knees and pressed his ear to the register.

The voice below the floor was wet, patient, intimate.

Why did you leave me under there?

He did not remember pulling the vent cover free. He remembered only the dust, the dark shaft between the walls, and the smell rising up from the house like a truth finally warming.

The whisper was not in the duct.

It was beneath him.

Beneath the boards.

Beneath the foundation where he had crawled with a shovel and a flashlight and convinced himself that earth accepted everything equally.

Martin went outside, circled the house, and yanked open the crawl-space hatch with shaking hands.

His flashlight beam cut across plastic sheeting, cinder blocks, damp soil.

The tarp was gone.

In the narrow darkness under the house, something inhaled.

Then Elena whispered from directly behind him, close enough for her breath to stir the hair at his neck.

"You forgot I know this house better than you do."
