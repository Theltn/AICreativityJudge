When they strapped Daniel Reeve into the chair, he kept his eyes on the witness room glass and tried to count the faces behind it. Numbers felt solid. Numbers did not lie.

Eight witnesses. Two guards. The prison chaplain with his folded hands. Warden Bell, standing straight as a fence post. And in the back, almost hidden by the reflection of fluorescent light, a woman in a gray coat Daniel did not recognize.

"Any final statement?" the warden asked.

Daniel swallowed against the dry cotton in his throat. "I didn't kill Mayor Whitcomb."

The ritual moved on around him. Leather buckles tightened. The cap came down. Somewhere a clerk noted the time.

Daniel listened to the hum of current gathering in the walls. He had spent six years telling anyone who would listen that the mayor's murder had been arranged by men richer than law. Six years watching appeals vanish. Six years becoming a story people used when they wanted to argue whether justice was merciful.

The executioner pulled the switch.

Nothing happened.

For one absurd second Daniel thought this was death: silence, waiting, no body at all. Then the overhead lights snapped off. The witness room vanished. The entire prison inhaled in the dark.

Someone shouted. Metal doors clanged open and shut in chaotic sequence as the locking system failed. An alarm tried to start, coughed once, and died.

"Generator!" Bell barked.

"Not responding!" a guard yelled back.

Footsteps pounded. Somewhere far down the block came the first scream.

Hands fumbled at Daniel's restraints. He jerked, expecting pain, but a woman's voice said sharply, "Hold still if you want to leave here alive."

The gray coat.

She cut the wrist strap, then the chest. "My name is Mara Whitcomb," she said.

He froze. The mayor's daughter.

"You came to watch me die?"

"I came because my father kept a backup of everything." She shoved the death cap off his head. "The blackout is mine. So is the data dump hitting every newspaper in the state right now."

In the darkness, the prison no longer felt like a place of law. It felt like a machine stripped open, gears visible and ugly.

"You knew?" Daniel asked.

"Two weeks ago." Her voice was steady, furious. "He wasn't murdered by a drifter with debts. He was killed by his business partners after he threatened to expose a contract scheme. Then they paid for your conviction to close the story."

Boots thundered toward them.

Mara sliced the final strap. "Can you walk?"

Daniel slid from the chair, legs buckling, then caught himself on the armrest that had nearly become his last horizon.

"Why help me?"

She looked toward the witness glass, now only a black mirror. "Because if they restore power before you are gone, they'll kill you in the confusion and call it procedure."

The door to the chamber burst open. In the dark, a gun flashed once. Bell cursed as the shot went wild.

Mara grabbed Daniel's hand and dragged him through the service corridor behind the execution room, a passage no condemned man was meant to see. All around them the prison roared with panicked men and failed systems.

Behind that noise, Daniel heard something stranger: the sound of a story changing.

By morning the evidence would be public, the governor cornered, the warden under arrest. His name would move from obituary draft to wrongful conviction.

But in the black heart of the prison, barefoot and half-burned by fear, Daniel understood only this:

The switch had been thrown.

The lights had gone out.

And for the first time in six years, the state had missed.
