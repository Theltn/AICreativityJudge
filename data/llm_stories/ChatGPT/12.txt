The Khelt invasion should have lasted six hours.

That was the estimate in the recovered battle doctrine after the war: neutralize orbital defenses in eighteen minutes, erase military coordination in ninety, force planetary surrender before local populations understood the scale of the event. Against most species, this had worked beautifully. The Khelt were methodical, technologically superior, and so convinced of the relationship between those two facts that they had stopped imagining alternatives.

They made three mistakes on Earth.

The first was shutting down the internet.

Their electronic warfare package vaporized satellites, cooked fiber trunks, and collapsed global communications in under eleven minutes. From orbit it looked elegant. On the ground it created eight billion angry primates with no distraction, no central narrative, and a sudden need to improvise local solutions.

The second mistake was assuming destroyed infrastructure stayed destroyed.

Humans treated infrastructure as suggestion even in peacetime. By sunset on the first day, mechanics in Lagos were mounting anti-armor rockets onto irrigation tractors. A high school robotics team in Osaka turned warehouse drones into guided munitions using gaming controllers and spare phone cameras. In Minnesota, a woman named Debra Hines got a Khelt scout walker running on biodiesel and used it to tow a hospital across a flooded highway.

The third mistake was giving us their technology intact.

Khelt weapons were elegant, sealed systems with no user-serviceable parts and no expectation that anyone outside trained military castes would touch them. This was incomprehensible to humanity, a species that had spent centuries looking at every object it encountered and asking three questions:

What does it do?

What else could it do?

What happens if I take it apart?

Within seventy-two hours, captured plasma lances had become cutting tools, generators, improvised railguns, and, in one regrettable but effective case outside Sao Paulo, a pressure cooker. A university machine shop in Delhi printed adapters that let alien power cells run desalination rigs. Farmers in Argentina wired targeting arrays to weather balloons. Somebody in rural Kansas mounted a Khelt gravitic plate under a grain silo and accidentally built the world's first agricultural aircraft.

The Khelt could not process this. Their intercepted battlefield traffic grew steadily more distressed.

ENEMY UTILIZING BREACH FOAM AS STRUCTURAL MATERIAL.

ENEMY HAS TURNED SCOUT CADAVERS INTO DECOYS.

ENEMY APPEARS TO BE MODIFYING OUR MINEFIELD FOR RECREATIONAL USE.

That last report referred to children in Poland using defused smart mines as sleds.

By week two the invasion had lost technological asymmetry. By week three, it had lost morale. Human resistance was not unified, disciplined, or aesthetically consistent, but it was omnivorous. We consumed advantage, metabolized it into junkyard miracles, and sent it back faster than doctrine could update.

When the Khelt ambassador finally requested terms, he asked a question historians still quote.

"How are you this adaptable?"

General Alvarez, who had once repaired helicopters with fencing wire and prayer, looked at the alien across the table and shrugged.

"Because," she said, "nothing on this planet has ever worked the way it was advertised."

The war ended nine days later.

The age of reverse-engineered starflight began the following Tuesday.
