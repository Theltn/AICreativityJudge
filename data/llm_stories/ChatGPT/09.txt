On December 30, exactly one hundred years after the papers had announced Grigori Rasputin finally dead, the Pacific gave him back to San Francisco.

He surfaced at dawn beside Pier 39, trailing kelp and black water, while the sea lions screamed and tourists lifted their phones in automatic worship. At first everyone thought it was performance art. The city had trained them well. If a bearded man in a ruined silk shirt emerged from the bay muttering in Russian, it was probably immersive theater or a startup launch.

Then he walked across the waterlogged planks leaving footprints that steamed in the cold.

By noon he was on every screen in America.

The historians argued first. The Church issued denials. The Russian consulate requested calm. A panel on cable news spent forty-three minutes discussing whether Rasputin's return constituted foreign interference.

Rasputin ignored all of them.

He moved uphill through the city as if he knew where he was going, though he paused often to stare at glass towers and self-driving buses with an expression of almost vulgar delight. "Magic made lazy," he told a reporter in heavily accented English after apparently learning the language in six hours. "Bad for saints. Good for drunks."

Crowds followed him through North Beach, over Nob Hill, into Chinatown. People laughed, prayed, livestreamed, and tried to sell him branded hoodies. He accepted a coffee, blessed a parking meter, and told a venture capitalist that his aura smelled of boiled coins.

By evening the police had established a perimeter around Grace Cathedral, where Rasputin finally stopped.

He stood on the steps dripping seawater onto the stone and looked west, toward the ocean that had delivered him.

"You all think I come back for revenge," he announced.

His voice carried farther than it should have. Cars idled in silence. Even the helicopters above seemed to hang still in the fog.

"Always revenge with you modern people. Such small imagination."

Someone in the crowd shouted, "Then why are you here?"

Rasputin smiled, and for a second the smile looked older than the nation around him.

"Because this is where the crack begins."

The mood changed then. A thousand phones kept recording, but the laughter thinned.

Rasputin turned and pressed his wet palm against the cathedral doors. Frost spread through the wood in branching silver veins.

"For one hundred years," he said softly, "I have been very difficult to kill. In the places below water, one learns why."

The bells overhead began ringing without hands.

Out in the bay, every ship horn answered.

People would later disagree about what they saw next. Some swore the fog drew itself into human shapes kneeling along the hills. Others claimed the ocean retreated from the shore all at once, exposing black stairways descending into impossible depth. One woman on the livestream feed only screamed, over and over, "There's a city under the water."

Rasputin faced the crowd like a priest about to begin service.

"Do not fear," he said, which was the least useful sentence anyone had ever spoken.

Then he laughed, threw open the cathedral doors, and the sound that came from inside was not a choir, not wind, but a great patient tide inhaling after a century of waiting.
