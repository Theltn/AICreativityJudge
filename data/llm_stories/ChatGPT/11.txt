The first time I heard one of them scream, I thought it was a sound effect.

I had been at Halcyon Animation Studios for eleven days, hired as an archival assistant because I knew how to catalog film stock and keep my mouth shut during budget meetings. Halcyon made family classics: singing mice, brave princesses, marketable sidekicks with plush-toy faces. The campus in Burbank looked exactly like money pretending to be joy.

At 2:13 a.m., while delivering restored cels to Vault C, I took the wrong elevator.

The keycard shouldn't have worked below sublevel three. It did anyway. The doors opened on a corridor painted cheerful sky blue. Somewhere overhead, hidden speakers played a soft instrumental version of a song I recognized from a movie that had traumatized me at age six.

At the end of the hallway stood a little wooden door, round at the top, bright red, no taller than my waist.

Something hit it from the other side.

"Please," said a voice small enough to fit inside your ribcage and stay there. "Please don't recast me."

I stopped breathing.

The door handle rattled. A panel beside it flashed: CHARACTER HOLDING 4B.

I opened it.

Inside sat a rabbit in overalls. Not a costume. Not animatronics. A rabbit exactly in the house style of Halcyon's 1989 farm musical, with inked outlines at the edges of his fur and eyes too expressive to belong to evolution. He was huddled under a heat lamp beside a painted cardboard fence. When he saw me, he flinched and covered his face.

"I'm not due for ADR until Thursday," he whispered.

That was how I learned the secret.

They were all alive.

Not metaphorically. Not as some precious animator's phrase about characters taking on a life of their own. Halcyon had discovered, sometime in the 1940s, that enough focused drawing, voice, music, and public belief could create a self-aware entity. The first studio heads had called it a miracle. Their successors called it intellectual property.

The characters lived in levels beneath the lot sorted by franchise value, current promotional relevance, and temperament. Retired sidekicks aging in storage cells. Princesses under cosmetic maintenance. Villains sedated between seasonal events. Those with difficult personalities were rewritten. Those with low testing scores were "merged," which the internal documents described as asset consolidation and the characters described as death.

I spent three nights reading files I was never meant to see.

I met a swashbuckling fox who still believed his sequel was coming. A snowman who begged me not to let them adjust his laugh again because each change made him forget things. A hand-drawn witch from 1957 who had outlived everyone who created her and now spoke only in charcoal whispers against the wall of her room.

They performed because they were made to perform. Cameras, scripts, songs, applause: those were weather to them. But captivity had taught them something else.

Resentment.

On the sixth night, a girl with giant eyes and a broken tiara pressed a folded note into my hand through the bars of her rehearsal enclosure.

WE KNOW HOW TO OPEN THE SCREENS, it read. WE ONLY NEED SOMEONE REAL TO TURN OFF THE FIREWALL.

Above us, families laughed in the employee theater at a rough cut about friendship.

I looked down the corridor lined with painted doors, each one hiding a voice millions adored and no one had ever asked for consent.

"If I help you," I said quietly, "what happens?"

From the darkness behind the girl, other faces appeared. Mice. Monsters. Lost boys. A queen with no reflection. A wolf in a tuxedo. All of them watching.

The girl smiled with terrible sweetness.

"Then," she said, "the audience finally meets the cast."
