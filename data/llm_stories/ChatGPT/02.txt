By the time the humans appeared, the city of Gallow had been dead for one hundred and twelve years, and that was precisely why it functioned.

No one hurried in Gallow. No one lied, because deceit took energy. No one hoarded, because greed had once been tied to heartbeat and hormones and a warm flood of chemicals now mercifully gone. The dead rose each dawn, if dawn was the word for the pale silver light that filtered through constant cloud, and they worked because work needed doing. A mason whose jaw hung loose repaired walls. A seamstress with one eye stitched coats from grave linen. Children who had died children attended school forever and learned, patiently, from teachers with worm-eaten hands.

They had built a peaceful civilization from the ruins of appetite.

Then a fishing boat drifted into the harbor with three living people aboard.

At first, the council assumed illness. The strangers were flushed, sweating, wildly expressive. Their chests fluttered. Their eyes darted from rooftop to alley with feral calculation. One of them, a man with a beard, wept because the soup offered to him was "too hot" and then accused the serving girl of poisoning him.

"They are suffering from an advanced pulse condition," said Doctor Mera, placing cool fingers against the man's throat and recoiling at the frantic throb there. "I have never felt anything so aggressive."

The humans were quarantined in the old music hall. That lasted three days.

On the fourth, they broke a window, stole blankets, and bit a watchman.

The bite did not spread life, not exactly. But afterward the watchman developed symptoms: restlessness, suspicion, sudden private ambition. He accused his sister of taking more lamp oil than her share. He hid bread under his floorboards though he did not need bread at all. When confronted, he shouted that he had a right to security.

The word itself spread faster than the condition.

Security. Property. Desire. Fear.

Soon other citizens began changing. They locked doors. They formed opinions about rank. An undertaker punched a baker over a wheelbarrow. A child who had never before considered the future began sobbing over the thought that she might lose it.

The council convened in emergency session.

"If this continues," rasped Mayor Holt, whose skull showed white through his cheek, "we will become as we were before death improved us."

No one wished to speak the obvious solution. The humans sat below, in their cell, alive in every obscene way: hearts battering, lungs swelling, minds flashing from terror to hope to schemes too quick to track.

Mera descended alone.

The youngest human, a woman no older than twenty, looked up through the bars. "Please," she whispered. "We didn't know. We thought this place was abandoned."

Mera studied her. Life was astonishingly loud. Every movement announced itself with need.

"In our histories," Mera said, "the old world ended because people believed hunger made them special. Then we died, and found that without hunger we could still build, still care, still sing. Better, even."

The woman pressed trembling hands together. "We can learn."

Above them, a bell rang. Somewhere in the city, another fight had broken out.

Mera unlocked the cell.

Not to free them.

To enter with a tray of tea, paper, and ink.

"Then you will start," she said, sitting across from the outbreak, "by writing down every terrible idea your living body gives you before it infects anyone else."

The humans stared.

It was a slow cure, and an imperfect one. But the dead of Gallow had centuries, and patience had always been easier without a pulse.
