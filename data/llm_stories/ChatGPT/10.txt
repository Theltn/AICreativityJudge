Every Thursday in C Block, six convicted felons gathered in the library annex to kill goblins.

That was what the guards thought, anyway.

The prison chaplain had started the Dungeons and Dragons group as a rehabilitation initiative after reading an article about empathy and structured imagination. Warden Fisk approved it because it kept difficult men quiet for three hours and produced excellent photos for the oversight board.

So every Thursday, under flickering fluorescent lights, Hector the forger became a dwarf cleric. Birdie, who had once emptied armored trucks with homemade thermite, spoke in a falsetto as an elven bard. Luis the locksmith rolled dice with holy concentration. Even Mack, serving life for a murder he never discussed, leaned over the map like a schoolboy.

At the head of the table sat Benji Cole, sentenced twelve years for fraud and, more importantly, former assistant manager of three escape rooms and one regional theater.

Benji was the Dungeon Master.

"The corridor bends east," he said, pencil tapping the graph paper. "Two guards at the iron gate. One is sleepy. One is arrogant."

Luis frowned theatrically. "Can I inspect the hinges?"

"Roll for perception."

The dice clattered. Around the table, heads bent in apparent concentration while the watchers in the booth saw only fantasy nonsense: dragons, traps, arguments about torches.

What they did not see was the code.

East meant laundry wing. Iron gate meant service door. Sleepy and arrogant identified guards by habit, not name. Spell slots marked timing windows. Monster counts translated to cameras. When Birdie asked whether his bard could "carry the halfling in a violin case," everyone at the table knew he was confirming the dimensions of the linen carts.

The game had taken nine months to build. That was Benji's real talent. Not lying, exactly. Architecture. He made false walls people wanted to live inside.

Tonight was Session Twenty-Seven, and the party had finally reached the Obsidian Tower.

Benji described the central chamber in loving detail. "There is a shaft descending into darkness. Very narrow. Probably impossible for anyone broader than, say, Luis."

Luis scratched his chin. "Lucky me."

Mack asked the necessary question. "And what's at the bottom?"

Benji looked up.

"Freedom," he said.

No one smiled. Not even Birdie.

The plan depended on three things: the thunderstorm forecast after midnight, the temporary blind spot in the west corridor where a camera had been replaced but not calibrated, and Officer Grady's weekly habit of taking an extra seven minutes in the staff bathroom after lights-out. Benji knew the last because Grady always talked too much during snack distribution, and nobody listened to men in uniform more carefully than men wearing numbers.

At 9:56 p.m., the session ended. Character sheets were collected. Dice were counted. The guards laughed about nerds and escorted them back to their cells.

At 12:14 a.m., thunder shook the roof.

At 12:17, Luis slipped through the maintenance hatch behind the laundry machines.

At 12:19, Birdie started a fight loud enough to pull both block guards.

At 12:21, Mack bent the service grate exactly where Benji's map said the rust had spread.

By dawn, four men were gone.

Benji was not one of them.

When Warden Fisk stormed into the library later and tore apart the campaign notes, he found only dungeon maps, monster names, and the final unfinished line in Benji's careful handwriting:

The Dungeon Master stays behind to keep the dragon busy.

In Cell 214, Benji listened to the alarms and closed his eyes.

For the first time in years, somebody else had reached the exit.
