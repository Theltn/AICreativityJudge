Sir Aldren expected fire, treasure, and a final speech he could later improve for the songs.

Instead, when he entered the dragon's hall with sword drawn and shield raised, he found his daughter sitting on a basalt ledge holding claws.

Not captive. Holding hands.

Lysa turned at the scrape of his boots. "Father," she said, with the mild annoyance of a child interrupted at needlework. "You were not supposed to come through the front tunnels."

Across from her, a young dragon roughly the size of a carriage lifted his head from where it had been resting near her shoulder. His scales were green-black and not yet ridged with age. Around one horn Lysa had tied a blue ribbon Aldren recognized from her childhood braids.

Aldren's sword point dipped.

"Release her," he told the beast automatically.

The dragon blinked. "I don't think that's the issue here."

For a moment no one moved. Behind them, deeper in the mountain, the vast breathing of the elder dragon rolled like distant surf.

Lysa stood. She was wearing travel leathers beneath her cloak and the stubborn expression she had inherited from her mother. "I was going to tell you."

"You vanished from the keep three days ago!"

"I left a note."

"You left a drawing of a flower."

"It was our meeting place."

The young dragon cleared his throat, producing a small puff of embarrassed smoke. "In fairness, sir, it was a very specific flower."

Aldren stared at him. "Do not 'sir' me in my own rescue."

Lysa stepped between them before either male could commit to a poor decision. "His name is Serrik."

"I do not care what his name is."

"I think you should," said a new voice.

The elder dragon uncoiled from the shadows, enormous as a chapel, gold eyes opening one by one in the dark. Aldren had fought men, boars, and once a bear with a spear snapped in half. Nothing in his long career had prepared him for being judged by a creature older than his kingdom.

"Your daughter and my son appear attached," the elder dragon said. "We are discussing terms."

"Terms?" Aldren repeated.

"Marriage terms," Lysa said.

He nearly dropped the sword.

What followed was not a battle but, in many ways, something more harrowing: negotiation. Aldren learned that Lysa had been slipping into the mountain for months to study ancient languages with Serrik, who had grown bored of hoarding and curious about poetry. Serrik learned that human fathers considered "I promise I would never intentionally incinerate her" an insufficient dowry statement. The elder dragon demanded assurances against knightly harassment. Aldren demanded that no future grandchildren be raised in active lava.

Hours passed.

At last, exhausted, Aldren sheathed his sword.

Lysa watched him with guarded hope. Serrik held very still, smoke curling nervously from his nostrils.

"If I forbid this," Aldren said slowly, "you will do it anyway."

"Yes," said his daughter.

"Almost certainly," said the dragon.

Aldren rubbed a hand over his face. Somewhere in the hall, treasure glimmered with the uselessness of old expectations.

"Then," he muttered, "we will do this properly, and the bards can choke on it."

Lysa laughed first. Serrik after her, a strange bright rumble that made sparks dance in the air.

Outside, the kingdom still believed a knight had entered the dragon's abode to rescue his child.

By dawn, Aldren knew the truth.

He had come to negotiate with his future in-laws.
