The cure was announced on a Monday, monetized by Wednesday, and militarized before the month was out.

Dr. Sora Vale called it Telomeric Stabilization Therapy, though nobody used the name for long. The news preferred simple phrases. Death cure. End of aging. The forever shot.

Technically, it was narrower than that. People could still die from trauma, disease, violence, stupidity, and the ordinary chaos of being embodied. But if a body remained intact and treatable, natural decline simply stopped. Organs maintained. Cells repaired. Time ceased acting as executioner.

At first the world celebrated exactly as you would expect.

Markets exploded. Religions fractured. Eighty-year-olds booked marathon vacations. Cosmetic companies collapsed. Retirement law became a joke by lunchtime. Every government on Earth promised equitable access while quietly assigning armed guards to their national stockpiles.

Then the demographers released the numbers.

Earth could not sustain an indefinitely growing human population if no one died naturally. Not with water stress, crop limits, urban density, and ecosystems already straining. Give the cure to everyone and within a century the math turned savage. There was no policy elegant enough to evade it.

So humanity invented a new category of murder and gave it a bureaucratic name.

The Continuance Office handled births, deaths, and balance. If someone received the cure, someone else, somewhere, had to forgo it. At first the sacrifice was abstract, delayed into future quotas and statistical ceilings. That fiction lasted six months. Then waiting lists stretched into generations, and every treatment became visibly linked to a missing place at the table.

My mother got her offer the year I turned thirty-two.

She had heart failure, not yet fatal but trending there. Without the cure she might have had five years. With it, perhaps five hundred. The letter from the Office arrived in a gray envelope with no insignia, as if discretion could soften arithmetic.

ELIGIBILITY CONFIRMED.
CONTINUANCE SLOT RESERVED.
ASSOCIATED REPRODUCTION IMPACT: 3.1 POTENTIAL FUTURES.

"What does that even mean?" she asked, reading it at our kitchen table.

It meant that somewhere in the future, more than three people who might have lived would not. Not named people. Not yet. Possibilities.

That was how the system survived public outrage: it kept the victims blurry.

Mom set the letter down. "Your sister thinks I should do it."

"What do you think?"

She looked toward the window, where children in the courtyard below were drawing hopscotch grids in sidewalk chalk, each square a bright little claim on tomorrow.

"I think," she said slowly, "that natural death was cruel. But at least it wasn't administrative."

For weeks we talked in circles. Philosophers filled the networks. Protesters demanded lotteries, merit systems, parental limits, mandatory service, off-world colonization plans. Billionaires bought black-market doses and private militias. Clinics were bombed. Birth licenses became wedding gifts and political weapons.

On the morning of her appointment, my mother put on lipstick for the first time in months and asked me to drive.

Halfway there she told me to pull over by the river.

We sat in silence while barges moved through the gray water and a flock of birds turned in the sky like a single thought changing direction.

"I've had seventy-one years," she said. "That is not enough, but it is not nothing."

I knew then what she had decided, and hated her for a moment with the childish fury grief reserves for the noble.

"You don't owe the future your death."

"No," she said. "But I do owe it honesty."

She tore the appointment letter in half and let the pieces rest in her lap.

When we drove home, the world was still full of people trying to solve infinity with paperwork.

My mother spent her remaining years planting fruit trees in abandoned lots.

She said if we insisted on rationing time, we should at least leave sweetness for the ones who came after.
