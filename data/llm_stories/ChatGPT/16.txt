Agent Mara Singh slid a paper cup of water across the metal table and tried not to look at the blood on my shirt.

It was difficult blood to ignore.

Some of it had dried in a fan pattern across the interrogation room window where my head had burst open ten minutes earlier. Some still ran in dark threads from my hairline, though the hole in my forehead had already sealed. The bullet, flattened and warm, sat between us in an evidence bag.

The FBI had many protocols.

None covered this.

"For the record," Singh said, voice carefully neutral, "state your name."

"Daniel Mercer."

"And you understand why you're being detained."

"Because I shot myself in front of twelve federal agents and ruined your carpet."

Her jaw tightened. Behind the one-way glass, silhouettes shifted. There would be phones ringing out there. Directors being dragged from dinner. Someone from Behavioral Analysis already drafting a memo no one wanted to sign.

Singh clicked her pen. "Why did you do it?"

"Because no one returns my emails."

That almost broke her composure. Almost.

She opened a file folder. Inside were printouts of my messages to the Bureau over the past seven years: reports of impossible deaths, impossible sightings, a man appearing in photographs across two centuries without aging a day. Each complaint had been escalated exactly nowhere.

"You could have submitted evidence."

"I did. You called it fabricated."

"And today was supposed to change that."

I leaned back, feeling bone knit beneath skin with the familiar pressure of returning. Immortality hurts every time. Anyone who tells you otherwise has never had an eye grow back under fluorescent lighting.

"Today was triage," I said. "Now you'll listen."

Singh studied me for a long moment. She was good at this. Most interrogators reach for dominance when the room tilts sideways. She reached for structure.

"Let's assume," she said, "that what we witnessed was genuine. Explain it."

"I can't die permanently. Haven't been able to since 1683. Disease, drowning, hanging, gunshots. Always comes back."

She wrote that down, which I respected.

"Why contact the FBI now?"

I looked toward the glass. Toward the watching shapes. Toward the nation behind them, large and armed and always slightly late.

"Because I'm not the only one."

That landed.

Singh's pen stopped moving.

"There's another immortal," I said. "Name changes every generation. Currently using Elias Vane. He enjoys institutions because they hide him. Hospitals. Militaries. Intelligence work. Anywhere records can be sealed and bodies can disappear into procedure."

"You expect me to believe you've spent three centuries hunting one man?"

"No. I expect you to believe I've spent three centuries failing."

Outside, someone tapped the glass. Singh ignored it.

"Why not kill him?"

I laughed. The sound came out thinner than I intended. "Agent, if I knew how, I would not be in your building."

She looked at the bullet bag, then back at me. For the first time, suspicion gave ground to something more useful.

"What does he want?"

"What everyone wants once time stops limiting them," I said. "Control enough to make boredom impossible."

The room fell quiet.

Singh closed the file. "If I walk out that door and tell my superiors this interview continues as a joint operation, they will ask why I trust you."

"Tell them you don't."

"Then why continue?"

I smiled through drying blood.

"Because he already works for one of you."
