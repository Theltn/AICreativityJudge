The ringing started three days after the accident.

At first, Lena thought it was ordinary tinnitus. The doctor in urgent care agreed. Airbag deployment, mild concussion, stress. Very common. Rest, hydration, less screen time. She went home with a pamphlet and a bruise shaped like a handprint across her chest where the seatbelt had saved her life.

By Thursday, the ringing had become unmistakable.

It wasn't a whine or a buzz. It was a phone.

Not the chirp of a modern ringtone, either. This was the clean metallic trill of an old landline, insistent and slightly formal, as if someone somewhere expected to be answered by a person in slippers.

It came at irregular hours. In the shower. At the grocery store. At 2:11 a.m. sharp, just behind her left ear.

Her sister Maya found her in the kitchen pressing both palms to the side of her head.

"Are you alright?"

Lena shut her eyes. Another ring bloomed through her skull, so clear she could almost feel the vibration in her teeth.

"No," she whispered. "It's ringing."

Maya's expression shifted from concern to careful concern, which was worse.

They went back to the doctor. Hearing tests. MRI. Neurology referral. Everyone asked whether Lena had been under emotional strain before the crash. No one had an answer for why the ringing seemed louder when she stood near mirrors.

On Sunday night, while brushing her teeth, she caught her reflection answering a phone.

It lasted half a second. In the mirror, her right hand was raised to her ear, fingers curled around a receiver that did not exist in the bathroom. Her reflected mouth moved.

Hello?

Lena spun so hard she knocked the cup into the sink.

The ringing stopped.

After that, she began noticing other differences. In reflective windows, her apartment looked furnished with older things. A rotary phone on the hall table. Floral wallpaper she had stripped years ago. In darkened screens, someone crossed rooms behind her with Lena's own gait and posture but the wrong haircut, the one she wore at nineteen.

The night she finally answered, she did it out of exhaustion.

The ringing had dragged her from sleep again. 2:11 a.m. The mirror over her dresser glimmered faintly in moonlight. In it, the other room waited, dim and old-fashioned, and on the small table beside the reflected bed sat a black telephone ringing furiously.

Lena stepped closer.

Her reflection was already there, hand hovering above the receiver.

"If I pick up," Lena said to the glass, "what happens?"

Her reflection looked at her with red-rimmed eyes.

"You remember," it mouthed.

The phone rang again.

Lena reached toward the mirror. Cold passed through her fingertips like water.

On the other side, her other self lifted the receiver.

The voice that came through was her own, from the night of the crash, raw with terror and glass. She heard tires. Rain. Maya screaming in the passenger seat.

Then the sound she had not remembered.

The second impact.

The one after the ambulance.

The one that should have killed her.

When Lena woke on the floor at dawn, the mirror was cracked from top to bottom. The ringing was gone.

So was every memory she had spent a week trying not to hear.

Maya had not survived the accident.

All that time, the call had been coming from the life Lena was refusing to answer.
