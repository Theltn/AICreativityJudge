The first sign that humanity was not alone came twelve light-hours past Pluto, when the scout alarms began singing in polite, descending tones.

Captain Elian Voss lifted his eyes from the navigation glass. Ahead of the Arkady's silver prow, empty space puckered inward like fabric pinched by invisible fingers. A line appeared there. Not a beam, not a planet, just a line: white, ruler-straight, stretching from nothing to nowhere.

"Report," he said.

"There is... a checkpoint," said Lieutenant Imani, and then, after a pause, "Sir, I realize how that sounds."

The line unfolded into structure. Black pylons rose from vacuum. Lamps bloomed atop them, warm and yellow, the color of old harbor lights. Between two pylons hung a booth no larger than a roadside toll station. It stood on nothing. Behind its cloudy window, a silhouette shuffled papers.

No one spoke.

Humanity had spent ninety years building the first interstellar ship. Children had been raised on documentaries about brave departures and infinite frontiers. No one had mentioned customs.

The booth door opened. A figure floated out in a dark coat with brass buttons and a cap tucked under one arm. It was roughly human in shape, if one ignored the extra set of elbows and the face that kept rearranging itself as though undecided.

It tapped the hull. The sound rang through the Arkady like a spoon against a glass.

"State vessel registry," came a voice over every speaker, crisp and bored.

Voss recovered first. "Interstellar Vehicle Arkady, launched from Earth, Sol system. We come in peace."

"Mm." The creature wrote something on a clipboard. "Purpose of departure?"

"Exploration."

"Do you have a transit permit?"

"A what?"

The border guard sighed with the profound fatigue of bureaucracy. "Transit permit. Migration declaration. Hazard disclosure. Memetic sanitation certificate. Any proof at all that your species is authorized to cross the nursery boundary?"

Imani whispered, "Nursery?"

The guard looked up. Its eyes became six tiny stars. "Yes. Solar systems of your class are protected incubators. Early tool-using civilizations develop there until they are fit for broader contact. Most do not reach the shell. Congratulations, by the way. It usually takes longer."

Voss stared past the being into the dark where the stars waited, suddenly less like distant fire and more like lit windows in a city he had never been told existed.

"You mean," he said slowly, "the entire galaxy has borders."

"Of course it does. You creatures are adorable." The guard flipped a page. "Any biological weapons on board?"

"No."

"Self-replicating machines?"

"Not intentionally."

"Religious schisms likely to destabilize neighboring systems?"

Voss hesitated.

The guard marked something. "I'll put down 'moderate.'"

Behind the bridge glass, the crew watched the universe shrink from myth into administration. It was humiliating. It was also, Voss felt with a rising thrill, the most wonderful thing he had ever seen. If there were forms, there were offices. If there were offices, there were roads. If there were roads, somebody had gone somewhere before.

"What happens if we don't have the permit?" he asked.

The guard tucked the clipboard under its arm. "Then I issue you a provisional visa, a packet of regulations, and a warning not to touch any singularities without supervision." It leaned closer to the glass. "First departures are mostly ceremonial. We just need to make sure you don't mistake the trade lanes for wilderness and blow yourselves up."

The booth lights turned green.

Far beyond, the stars seemed to shift, revealing currents and avenues hidden in the dark.

Captain Voss smiled despite himself. Humanity had not escaped the neighborhood.

It had just learned the neighborhood was infinite.
