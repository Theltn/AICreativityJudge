The children had never seen yellow before.

They knew the word, of course. Teacher Mara had shown them yellow in old books, where it appeared beside smiling suns and raincoats and flowers that no longer grew. But words taught underground were always thin things. The real world had been soot-black for seventeen years, ever since the fires in the northern oil fields climbed into the sky and never properly came down.

That was why Elias volunteered for surface duty on museum days.

There were only six children in the tram this morning, wrapped in patched coats, goggles too large for their faces. They sat in disciplined silence until the tram broke through the final lock and began its slow ascent toward the observatory dome. Then their silence became a pressure all its own, six small bodies leaning toward the future.

"Will it hurt?" asked Nia, the smallest.

"Only your eyes at first," Elias said. "Do not remove the goggles until I say."

She nodded solemnly and squeezed the handrail.

Above them, motors groaned. Bolts drew back. The hatch opened with a sound like a vault surrendering treasure.

Cold air rushed in, thin and sharp and carrying the smell of stone. The children stepped out onto the platform one by one. Around them stretched the old world: towers half buried in ash dunes, highways collapsed into gray ravines, the skeletons of wind turbines turning lazily over the dead plains.

And beyond all that, on the eastern horizon, a break in the clouds.

It was not much. A seam of gold no wider than a finger.

Elias told them to lift their goggles.

They did, and the platform filled with breathless sounds. Not speech. Something smaller and more honest than speech.

The gold brightened. It touched a bank of cloud and turned it to fire. The children stared as though someone had ripped open heaven and let all its hidden metals pour through.

"That's sunrise?" whispered Tomas.

"Part of one," Elias said.

The atmospheric veils had been thinning for years. Engineers said full daylight might return in another decade, perhaps less. The children would be adults by then. They might raise crops in open fields. They might cast shadows with no lamp above them.

Nia took one careful step to the rail. "Everything is so big."

It was. Underground, the world had been narrowed into pipes, bunks, lesson walls, ration lines. Up here, even ruin had grandeur. The children turned in slow circles, staring at the broken city glazed with new light, the glittering frost on antennae, the orange edge growing brighter with each heartbeat.

Behind Elias, Teacher Mara murmured, "Look at them. They grew up without hope. Without sun. Everything here must be amazing in their eyes."

Elias almost corrected her. Hope had existed underground, but it had been practical and rationed, parceled out in repair shifts and seed banks and air filters that kept running one more winter.

What the children lacked was not hope.

It was extravagance.

The world had finally given them some.

Nia tugged his sleeve. "Was it always this beautiful?"

Elias looked toward the horizon, where the first true spear of sunlight pierced the clouds and struck the observatory glass until the whole dome blazed.

"No," he said, smiling despite the ache in his throat. "Some days it was even better."
