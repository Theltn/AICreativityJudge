When I woke in a wheat field outside Rome, I had exactly three assets: a splitting headache, mud in both shoes, and Volume One of a physical 2012 Wikipedia print compilation I had bought ironically at a library sale.

The date, I learned by very patient gestures and one coin, was late in the year we would call 27 BC.

Which meant Gaius Octavius had not yet become Augustus, the Republic was dying with ceremony, and I was the only person within several thousand miles carrying an alphabetical summary of world history in a backpack.

At first I tried to survive quietly.

Quiet survival ended on day four when a local official demanded to know why my Latin sounded like "a drunk ghost," searched my belongings, and discovered that my book contained accurate portraits of Cleopatra, maps of Germania, and an article on aqueducts better than anything his engineers had ever seen.

Three arrests, one beating, and a very tense translation later, I found myself in a marble chamber explaining to Octavian's advisers why my strange codex listed the names of emperors who did not officially exist yet.

"You claim this is history?" asked Maecenas.

"A version of it," I said.

Octavian himself, cold and watchful as a blade laid on silk, turned pages with careful fingertips. He lingered over Augustus, over Pax Romana, over civil wars, over the footnotes about his image-making and reforms.

"This book says I become divine after death," he said.

"A lot of people become things after death," I replied before I could stop myself.

That nearly got me killed.

Instead, it got me employed.

For eleven months I served as a kind of tolerated oracle in a villa under guard, translating selected entries and omitting as much as I dared. I gave them practical knowledge where I could do the least damage: sanitation, concrete mixtures, warnings about lead pipes. I avoided gunpowder. I avoided the printing press. I absolutely avoided the section on Christianity, though one sleepless night I stared at the page for an hour and considered how easily a civilization could be kicked sideways.

The problem with possessing Wikipedia in the ancient world was not that people would worship it.

It was that powerful men understood it immediately as leverage.

Octavian wanted military futures. Trade routes. Which rivals would betray him, which poets outlived him, which provinces rebelled. He asked history not as philosophy but as procurement.

I began tearing pages out.

One at a time, hidden in the brazier after translating something harmless. Bison. Borneo. Byzantine architecture, too early to matter. It felt obscene, destroying a book to protect the future, but the future in that room had become something pliable and frightened.

Eventually Octavian noticed the gaps.

"You are editing destiny," he said.

"No," I told him. "I'm trying to leave it some room."

The final page I kept was Augustus. I read it alone that night by oil lamp, the paper already softening from use, and understood the ugly truth: history had always been written by survivors, and I was dangerously close to becoming one of them.

The next morning I burned the article.

When guards came for the book, they found only smoke, a few surviving pages on astronomy, and one very angry, suddenly less-informed ruler.

I do not know whether I improved Rome. I know only that I denied it certainty.

Given the empire, that may have been the kindest gift I had.
