By the time Warren Pike understood he had founded a real religion, there were already three hundred people in white linen chanting his slogan at sunset.

The slogan had begun as marketing.

Ascension House was supposed to be a simple operation: rent a desert compound, promise rich, unhappy people freedom from mortality, collect initiation fees, then postpone the "final departure" until some legal complication or cosmic sign demanded another round of donations. Warren had run wellness frauds before. He knew the type. People did not want truth. They wanted expensive certainty.

So he gave them certainty in tasteful packaging.

He spoke in a low voice about transcending the prison of flesh. He commissioned a logo featuring a staircase disappearing into stars. He sold premium robes, silver amulets, and a ten-thousand-dollar "priority place in the first wave." The desperate called him Shepherd. Warren preferred direct deposit.

The trouble began with Lenora Voss.

Lenora was seventy, sharp-eyed, and richer than anyone else at Ascension House. Warren expected her to be a mark. Instead she became a disciple, and then an evangelist, and then something far more dangerous: an organizer.

She made spreadsheets.

Within a month there were committees. Supply teams. Security rotations. A media strategy. Warren's vague sermons were transcribed, indexed, and discussed by candlelight as if they were scripture instead of improvised nonsense stitched together from self-help podcasts and a documentary about Tibetan sky burials.

He tried softening the message. "Ascension," he explained one evening, smiling under string lights, "is as much metaphor as event."

The crowd fell silent.

Lenora rose from the front row. "Shepherd," she said gently, "fear is natural before revelation. We are with you."

They applauded him for his bravery.

After that, postponement became impossible. The members sold houses. Cut off families. Signed over estates to the Foundation for Post-Mortal Transition, which Warren had created in a single drunken afternoon for tax reasons. Money flooded in. So did expectation.

He planned to run.

Then security took his passport.

"For your protection," Lenora said. "The world will not understand what you are about to give us."

Warren laughed at first. "Lenora, come on."

She did not laugh back. "You opened the door. Do not stand in it now."

The final ceremony was scheduled for the new moon. Three hundred followers gathered on the salt flats beyond the compound, each carrying a lantern. Drones hovered overhead to capture the historic crossing. Warren stood on the platform in ceremonial white, sweat running down his spine beneath the robes.

At the center of the stage was the vessel: a polished silver chalice he himself had once described as "the cup through which flesh releases its final claim."

He had expected theater.

He had not expected poison.

"What is in that?" he hissed to Lenora.

"Release," she said.

"Are you insane?"

Her expression remained almost tender. "No. Faithful."

Out beyond the stage, three hundred faces shone in lantern light, serene and trusting and terrifying. Warren saw, with the cold precision of a true coward, that they were beyond saving because they did not believe they required it.

He stepped back.

Security closed in.

"You can't make me drink that."

Lenora tilted her head. "You asked us for everything. We gave it. It would be rude not to lead."

The crowd began to chant his title.

Shepherd. Shepherd. Shepherd.

Warren looked at the chalice, then at the dark desert beyond the lights. For the first time since inventing Ascension House, he understood the one commodity he had never intended to sell.

Belief.

And once he had taken payment in that currency, there was no refund.
