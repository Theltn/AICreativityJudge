Mrs. Frizzle said, "Class, I may have made a tiny navigational error," in exactly the same voice she used for volcanoes, dinosaurs, and that regrettable incident with Arnold's spleen.

Normally, that tone meant adventure.

This time it meant the stars were wrong.

We had left on a simple field trip to observe a solar eclipse from above the cloud layer. The Magic School Bus had stretched into a glossy gold rocket, the seats grown harnesses, and Liz was wearing a helmet no one had buckled onto her. Standard Tuesday. We should have arced neatly into the upper atmosphere, watched the moon pass over the sun, and returned before lunch.

Instead, when the shadow crossed us, the sky opened.

Not split. Opened.

The darkness behind the eclipse was not empty space but depth, enormous and watching. The bus lurched forward as if hooked from the other side. Windows became black mirrors. The dashboard sprouted dials labeled with symbols that hurt to read.

"Please tell me this is the planetarium dimension," Phoebe whispered.

Mrs. Frizzle gripped the wheel hard enough to whiten her knuckles. I had never seen that before. "It is not the planetarium dimension."

Around us drifted things too large to identify. Ribbons of light bent at impossible angles. Great circular shadows moved behind greater circular shadows. The bus shrank, expanded, and became momentarily transparent. I saw Carlos as a diagram of bones and fear.

"Seatbelts, everyone!" yelled Mrs. Frizzle.

"We are already wearing them!" Dorothy Ann yelled back, which felt unfair under the circumstances.

The bus computer, usually cheerful, spoke in a voice made of layered echoes. "Route recalculation unavailable. Cartography insufficient. Reality porous."

Arnold began crying very quietly.

Mrs. Frizzle looked at us in the rearview mirror, all freckles and panic jammed into a smile. "Class, sometimes science takes us somewhere unexpected."

"This isn't science," I said before my better judgment could stop me.

Outside the window, something the size of a continent turned its pupil toward us.

The bus lights failed. For one second we floated in total dark except for the eclipse burning around us like a ring held in a giant hand. Then the emergency lights snapped on red.

Liz hissed.

"Right," said Mrs. Frizzle, too brightly. "New lesson plan. We are going to observe, hypothesize, and absolutely not attract attention."

The thing outside pressed closer. On the glass bloomed a pattern like frost, except it spelled all our names.

No one breathed.

The bus shuddered as if something vast had tapped it with a fingernail.

Mrs. Frizzle reached beneath the dashboard and pulled a lever none of us had ever seen. Yellow sparks raced through the cabin. The seats dissolved. The floor became liquid. The bus began changing shape around us with desperate speed: submarine, beetle, comet, cell, bird.

"What are you doing?" I shouted.

"Trying every ecosystem I know," she said. "If I can convince this place we belong to something smaller, it may let us go."

The pupil outside narrowed. The bus shrank again, now no larger than a dust mote drifting through cosmic black water.

For the first time in her life, Mrs. Frizzle sounded exactly as scared as we were.

"Class," she whispered, "take notes."

Then the universe swallowed the bus whole, and our field trip became the kind of lesson no curriculum was built to survive.
